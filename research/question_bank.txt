# AIRLINE PILOT INTERVIEW QUESTION BANK
# 210 Questions with Expected Answer Guidance
# Generated from research corpus: aviationinterviews.com, flightdeckfriend.com, emeraldcoastinterviewconsulting.com, passmyinterview.com, mypilotinterview.com, spitfireelite.com, YouTube transcripts (14 videos including United Airlines Chief of Hiring, 5 Airline Interviews Later, Airline Recruiters on Pilot Confidence), and the book "Checklist for Success: A Pilot's Guide to the Successful Airline Interview" (Cheryl Cage, 7th Ed.)

================================================================================
SECTION 1: PERSONAL / MOTIVATIONAL (35 questions)
================================================================================

Q: Tell me about yourself / why you want to become a pilot with our airline.
EXPECTED: Open with a brief personal/professional summary that is aviation-focused (not life history). Cover current role, key ratings and experience, and trajectory. End by connecting your background to THIS airline specifically. Keep to 90-120 seconds. The interviewer wants to see you are organized, confident, and have done your homework on the airline. Do not list random personal facts unrelated to aviation.
KEY POINTS:
- Current role and aircraft type
- Key certifications and total flight time
- Brief career trajectory (how you got here)
- Why this airline specifically (culture, fleet, reputation, values)
- Forward-looking statement about contributing to the airline
AVOID: Rambling life story or mentioning pay/schedule as primary motivation. Never bad-mouth previous employers.

Q: Why do you want to be a pilot?
EXPECTED: This is a passion/motivation question. Authentic answers resonate far more than rehearsed ones. Trace your genuine interest in aviation — an early inspiration, a formative flying experience, what draws you to it intellectually and professionally. Connect the passion to a sense of responsibility and professionalism, not just thrill-seeking. Interviewers are assessing long-term commitment.
KEY POINTS:
- Genuine origin story of aviation interest
- The intellectual/professional aspects that sustain your passion (e.g., systems knowledge, navigation, weather)
- Sense of responsibility and public trust in the role
- Long-term commitment to the career
- Specific moment or experience that crystallized the decision
AVOID: "I've always loved flying" with no substance. Mentioning money, travel perks, or lifestyle as primary drivers.

Q: How did you get interested in aviation?
EXPECTED: Personal and specific. A family member who flew, a flight as a child, an airshow, a military experience, or a career pivot. Be concrete — name the airplane, the location, the age. This is a brief story question, not a philosophical answer. Authenticity matters more than drama.
KEY POINTS:
- Specific inciting incident or influence
- Age and context to make it vivid
- How that spark led to action (first lesson, first solo, training path)
- Shows consistent commitment over time
AVOID: Generic non-answers. Keep it under 60 seconds.

Q: Tell me about our airline and what attracted you to us.
EXPECTED: Demonstrate genuine research. Cover: fleet type(s) and size, key hubs/bases, route network, culture/mission, recent news, financial health, and why it aligns with your career goals. Specific facts show real preparation. Mention speaking with current pilots if true. Connect the airline's attributes to your own values.
KEY POINTS:
- Fleet types and approximate size (number of aircraft)
- Key hubs and major routes
- Mission statement or stated core values
- A specific recent development (new aircraft order, new route, merger)
- Cultural or professional attribute that resonates with you personally
AVOID: Generic praise. Never say "because you're the biggest" or "best pay." Research must be specific.

Q: Why do you want to work for our airline?
EXPECTED: Similar to above but more personal. Align the airline's culture and values with your own professional identity. For Southwest: lean into the culture and fun-loving attitude. For Delta: emphasize professionalism and network. For United: emphasize global mission and diversity. Show you understand what makes this airline distinct from its competitors and why that distinction matters to you.
KEY POINTS:
- Specific cultural alignment (reference their stated values)
- Career development opportunity at this specific airline
- Fleet/route network fit with your goals
- Something a current employee told you (if applicable)
- Demonstrates you chose THEM, not just any airline
AVOID: "You have great benefits." Vague compliments. Any hint of using them as a stepping stone.

Q: What do you know about our airline?
EXPECTED: A structured knowledge overview: founding year and history, current CEO and key leadership, fleet composition (types, engines, seat configs, MTOW, VMO), number of aircraft, main hubs, major destinations, annual passengers, revenue, recent news, competitors, and pending aircraft orders. This is the homework question — it rewards preparation with a tangible signal.
KEY POINTS:
- Founded (year) and key historical milestones
- Current fleet types and approximate count
- Main hubs and geographic focus
- CEO/Director of Flight Operations/Chief Pilot by name
- One or two current news items (new aircraft, new route, merger, safety award)
AVOID: Guessing or saying "I know you're a great airline." Outdated information.

Q: What will you bring to this airline?
EXPECTED: Frame your answer around three or four genuine professional strengths relevant to the airline context: CRM skills, specific aircraft experience, prior career skills that transfer (military, customer-facing, leadership), commitment to safety culture, and longevity. Be specific — cite examples, not just adjectives. Show you've thought about what THEY need, not just what you have.
KEY POINTS:
- Specific technical or operational experience relevant to their fleet/ops
- A transferable professional skill (leadership, customer service, prior career)
- CRM and communication strengths with a brief example
- Safety culture commitment
- Long-term commitment to the airline
AVOID: Generic list of positive adjectives. "I'm hardworking and dedicated" with no evidence.

Q: What qualities are needed to become an effective leader?
EXPECTED: For cockpit leadership, cover: clear and concise communication, decisiveness, openness to crew input, calm under pressure, accountability, situational awareness, and leading by example. The answer should reflect both command authority and collaborative management — not a dictator or a pushover. Reference cockpit leadership specifically, not generic leadership theory.
KEY POINTS:
- Clear and concise communication
- Decisive when required; open to input otherwise
- Calm under pressure; maintains composure
- Accountability — takes responsibility when things go wrong
- Situational awareness — sees the big picture while managing immediate tasks
- Leads by example in preparation, professionalism, and safety culture
AVOID: Listing only hard-charging command traits (ignores CRM). Or listing only collaborative traits (ignores command authority). Balance matters.

Q: What qualities should a pilot possess / what makes a competent pilot?
EXPECTED: Cover the full spectrum: technical knowledge, situational awareness, procedural discipline, CRM skills, communication, continuous learning, sound judgment under pressure, and a commitment to safety above schedule. Avoid just listing technical skills — airlines care deeply about the human factors dimension.
KEY POINTS:
- Technical competence and knowledge currency
- Strong situational awareness (big picture thinking)
- Procedural discipline — follows checklists even under pressure
- Excellent communication with crew, ATC, cabin crew, dispatch
- Sound judgment and decision-making under pressure
- Commitment to continuous learning and improvement
AVOID: Focusing only on stick-and-rudder skills. Missing the CRM/human factors dimension.

Q: What makes a good First Officer?
EXPECTED: The FO role is about supporting the captain, speaking up professionally when safety requires it, being technically prepared, and being a reliable second pair of eyes. Key: assertive but not aggressive; supportive but not passive. Reference the authority gradient — too steep leads to accidents (CFIT), too shallow creates friction. Show you understand the balance.
KEY POINTS:
- Technical competence and proactive preparation
- Monitoring the captain's actions without second-guessing
- Willingness to speak up assertively when safety is at risk
- Supporting the captain's decisions once made, unless unsafe
- Managing threats and errors before they become problems
- Professionalism and crew harmony
AVOID: "I just do what the captain says." Or positioning yourself as someone who would challenge the captain at every turn.

Q: What makes a good airline pilot?
EXPECTED: Blend technical and human skills. Airlines often emphasize: consistent use of SOPs, continuous learning, excellent communication, sound judgment, resilience, and being a good crew member. The "airline pilot" framing also implies schedule adherence, professionalism, customer awareness, and representing the company brand.
KEY POINTS:
- Consistent SOP adherence and procedural discipline
- Excellent CRM and communication with all stakeholders
- Sound judgment especially when procedures don't cover the situation
- Commitment to continuous improvement and regulatory currency
- Professional conduct on and off the flight deck
- Customer awareness — you're part of the passenger experience
AVOID: Mentioning stick-and-rudder skills only. Missing the "brand ambassador" dimension of airline flying.

Q: What defines a professional airline pilot?
EXPECTED: Professionalism extends beyond technical skills. Cover: punctuality and preparation, consistent SOP adherence, professional appearance and conduct, positive representation of the airline, continuous learning, integrity (honest reporting, honest self-assessment), and responsibility to the traveling public. The answer should convey gravitas — this is a position of public trust.
KEY POINTS:
- Preparation: thorough pre-flight planning, systems knowledge current
- SOP adherence: follows checklists and procedures even without oversight
- Integrity: honest incident reporting, honest self-assessment
- Conduct: professional demeanor on and off duty
- Public responsibility: carries the trust of every passenger
- Continuous improvement: always learning, takes feedback well
AVOID: Defining professionalism only as appearance or attitude. The core is about standards and integrity.

Q: What is your leadership style?
EXPECTED: For aviation: describe a collaborative but decisive leadership approach. Show you brief your crew before the flight (setting expectations), invite input during planning, are decisive in time-critical situations, and debrief after. Reference specific techniques: crew briefing, authority gradient management, assertive communication. Don't just describe yourself in adjectives — describe your behaviors.
KEY POINTS:
- Brief the crew before departure: set tone, invite questions, establish communication norms
- Collaborative in planning phase; decisive in execution
- Create psychological safety for crew to speak up
- Consistent, fair, and predictable — crew knows what to expect
- Debrief after flights to identify improvements
- Adjust style based on crew experience level
AVOID: "I lead by example" with no specifics. Describing an autocratic style that would suppress crew input.

Q: What are your greatest strengths?
EXPECTED: Choose three or four strengths directly relevant to airline flying: situational awareness, disciplined preparation, CRM communication, technical depth, calm under pressure. Back each with a brief specific example. Don't just list adjectives — show the behavior behind the strength.
KEY POINTS:
- Choose strengths relevant to the role (not generic)
- Back each with a brief concrete example
- At least one technical and one human-factors strength
- Connect strengths to what the airline specifically needs
- 3-4 strengths is the right number
AVOID: Humble-bragging ("I work too hard"). Generic lists without evidence.

Q: What is your greatest weakness?
EXPECTED: Name a real but manageable weakness, show self-awareness, and demonstrate the active steps you're taking to address it. Aviation-appropriate examples: over-perfectionism causing slow decisions, difficulty delegating, taking on too much personal responsibility. DO NOT say "I have no weaknesses" or pick a fake strength.
KEY POINTS:
- A genuine, not fake weakness
- Self-awareness about why it's a weakness
- Concrete mitigation steps already in progress
- Evidence the mitigation is working
- Ends on a growth-oriented note
AVOID: "I'm a perfectionist" with no genuine self-reflection. A weakness that would disqualify you.

Q: What would your current employer say is your biggest weakness?
EXPECTED: Same as the previous question but framed from an employer perspective. Tests whether your self-assessment matches external perception. Pick a weakness your supervisor has actually mentioned. Show you received the feedback gracefully and are working on it.
KEY POINTS:
- Must be plausibly what a supervisor would actually say
- Shows you've received feedback and taken it seriously
- Demonstrates self-awareness and growth mindset
- Brief and specific
AVOID: Picking a non-weakness. Picking something so serious it raises red flags.

Q: How would your former supervisors describe you?
EXPECTED: Should align with your stated strengths. Choose two or three qualities you're confident previous supervisors would actually say: reliable, well-prepared, professional, strong CRM, quick learner. Add a brief anecdote if possible. Shows external validation of your self-image.
KEY POINTS:
- Specific qualities (not just "hard working")
- Grounded in actual feedback you've received
- Brief illustrative example or anecdote
- Aligns with your other answers (consistency is important)
AVOID: Implausible claims. Anything that contradicts how you've described yourself elsewhere.

Q: What are your favorite things about your current job?
EXPECTED: Aviation-specific answer: the intellectual challenge of weather decision-making, the privilege of professional flight, working with a skilled crew, the responsibility. Should feel authentic. Interviewers use this to gauge passion and fit. Be specific about what aspects you genuinely enjoy.
KEY POINTS:
- Specific, authentic aspects of the role you genuinely enjoy
- At least one that relates to crew/people
- Connects aviation passion to professional execution
- Can preview why you're ready to move to this next opportunity
AVOID: Schedule, pay, or travel benefits as primary favorites.

Q: What are your least favorite things about your current job?
EXPECTED: Be honest but measured. Acceptable answers: irregular schedule affecting family time, limited upgrade opportunities (especially if leaving a regional), single-pilot ops limiting CRM growth, aging fleet. Avoid sounding like you're complaining or disparaging the employer. Frame as a professional challenge.
KEY POINTS:
- A genuine, measured answer
- Framed professionally, not as a complaint
- Connects to why you're seeking this new opportunity
- No disparagement of current employer
AVOID: Sounding bitter. Making the interviewer wonder why you'd be any happier at the new airline.

Q: Where do you see yourself in 5 years?
EXPECTED: Show ambition aligned with the airline's career path. Type rating completion, building PIC experience, potentially check airman or line instructor role. Show you see THIS airline as a long-term home.
KEY POINTS:
- Type rating completion and proficiency
- Line experience and mastery of the operation
- Possible training or check airman role (show aspirations)
- Long-term commitment to this airline
- Continuing education or ratings if applicable
AVOID: Any answer that implies you're using this airline as a stepping stone. Vague non-answers.

Q: Where do you see yourself in 10 years?
EXPECTED: More long-term vision. Captain upgrade, check airman, international routes, leadership roles. Show your career arc aligns with the airline's growth. Demonstrate institutional loyalty and desire to contribute back.
KEY POINTS:
- Captain upgrade timeline (realistic for the airline)
- Check airman or training captain aspiration
- Contributing to the airline's culture and mentoring junior pilots
- Long-term institutional loyalty
AVOID: Mentioning other airlines. Any reference suggesting you'd leave.

Q: What are your long-term career goals?
EXPECTED: Comprehensive answer: progression to captain, potential training roles, staying current with evolving technology, contributing to safety culture, mentoring younger pilots. Should convey flying is your vocation.
KEY POINTS:
- Progression to captain as primary goal
- Training and mentoring role once experienced
- Staying technologically current
- Contributing to organizational safety culture
- Flying as a vocation, not just a job
AVOID: Vague or uninspiring answers.

Q: Why do you want to leave your current employer?
EXPECTED: Always frame as a positive pull toward the new opportunity, not a push away from the current one. Acceptable reasons: limited upgrade opportunity, desire for larger aircraft, more international routes, the specific culture of this airline. Never disparage current employer.
KEY POINTS:
- Framed as a positive pull toward new opportunity
- Specific professional advancement reason
- No disparagement of current employer
- Shows loyalty (note how long you've been there)
- Logical career progression narrative
AVOID: Any negative comment about current employer. Mentioning pay or schedule as primary drivers.

Q: What is your greatest professional achievement?
EXPECTED: Specific, concrete accomplishment. Best options: captain upgrade, handling a real emergency, obtaining a difficult type rating, leadership milestone. Use brief STAR format. The achievement should reflect professional maturity.
KEY POINTS:
- Specific and concrete
- Shows professional growth and initiative
- Preferably aviation-specific
- Brief STAR structure
- Reflect on what it meant and what you learned
AVOID: Something trivial. Something that happened to you rather than something you achieved.

Q: Describe your most memorable flying experience.
EXPECTED: Can be positive or challenging. What matters is it reveals your character and relationship with aviation. Options: first solo, type rating checkride, difficult weather, memorable passenger interaction, emergency handled well. Describe vividly but briefly, then tie it to professional values.
KEY POINTS:
- Vivid, specific detail
- Reveals character — passion, judgment, or resilience
- Ties to professional values
- Brief — 60-90 seconds max
AVOID: A story reflecting poor judgment or unsafe behavior.

Q: What do you consider your biggest challenge in training?
EXPECTED: Honest self-reflection. Common genuine answers: instrument scan development, CRM after single-pilot background, workload management, mastering automation. Show what the challenge taught you and how you overcame it.
KEY POINTS:
- A genuine training challenge
- How you identified and diagnosed it
- The specific steps you took to overcome it
- What it taught you about your learning process
- How it made you a better pilot
AVOID: Claiming you had no challenges.

Q: What would you do if you lost your medical certificate?
EXPECTED: Shows resilience and multi-dimensionality. Pursue ground instructor, dispatcher, or simulator instructor certification; stay connected to aviation through instructing or safety roles; explore Special Issuance/AME consultation if medically appropriate.
KEY POINTS:
- Acknowledge the emotional difficulty honestly
- Pivot to remaining in aviation in a different capacity
- Ground instructor, simulator instructor, dispatcher, safety officer
- Explore Special Issuance/AME process if applicable
AVOID: "I can't imagine that" with no plan.

Q: How do you stay organized?
EXPECTED: Aviation answer: systematic pre-flight planning using checklists, logbook management, regulatory currency tracking, EFB management. Also applies to scheduling personal life around irregular aviation schedule.
KEY POINTS:
- Checklists and standard flows for all repetitive tasks
- Electronic flight bag management
- Logbook accuracy and regular review
- Currency tracking (medical, ratings, recurrent training)
- Communication systems with family for schedule management
AVOID: Vague generalities. Failing to connect to flight-specific contexts.

Q: How do you prevent procrastinating?
EXPECTED: Pre-briefing weather the night before, reviewing aircraft performance data ahead of time, arriving early, completing paperwork promptly. Show professional discipline applied proactively.
KEY POINTS:
- Task prioritization: know what must be done before what
- Time buffer: always allow more time than you think you need
- Pre-planning: weather, route, NOTAM review done night before when possible
- Checklists for repetitive planning tasks
AVOID: Generic "I write lists" answer not connected to aviation context.

Q: Tell me about the typical day of a pilot.
EXPECTED: Walk through a realistic duty day: pre-report prep (weather, NOTAMs, ATIS), crew brief, pre-flight inspection, pushback and engine start, taxi, departure, en-route monitoring, arrival briefing, approach, landing, post-flight paperwork, rest during layover. Show both systematic professional nature and genuine enthusiasm.
KEY POINTS:
- Pre-flight: weather brief, NOTAM check, ATIS, weight and balance
- Crew brief: CRM norms, weather threats, expectations
- Walk-around inspection
- Each flight phase (taxi, departure, en-route, arrival, landing)
- Post-flight: paperwork, ACARS, debrief if needed
AVOID: Making it sound routine or boring.

Q: What challenges does aviation / this airline face over the next 10 years?
EXPECTED: Demonstrate industry awareness. Key challenges: pilot shortage/surplus dynamics, fuel costs and sustainability, automation/AI integration, NextGen/SESAR, climate-related weather impacts, cybersecurity, single-pilot operations debate, electric/hybrid aircraft.
KEY POINTS:
- Pilot supply and demand dynamics
- Sustainability and decarbonization (SAF, electric aircraft)
- Automation and AI: autonomous systems, single-pilot operations debate
- Cybersecurity: increasingly networked aircraft and operations
- NextGen/SESAR: modernizing airspace management
AVOID: Only mentioning one challenge. Missing the sustainability dimension.

Q: How do you maintain work-life balance?
EXPECTED: Aviation scheduling is inherently irregular — acknowledge this honestly. Intentional quality time when off, exercise and health habits, family communication and buy-in, pursuit of hobbies, knowing when to call fatigue if genuinely impaired.
KEY POINTS:
- Intentional quality time when at home
- Regular exercise and health maintenance
- Family communication — they understand and support the lifestyle
- Hobbies that create true mental decompression
- Willingness to use fatigue mitigation options when genuinely tired
AVOID: "Aviation comes first" (suggests no personal life). "It's easy" (not credible).

Q: Why is it important for a pilot to have strong communication skills?
EXPECTED: Multi-dimensional: cockpit communication (CRM), ATC (precise and unambiguous), cabin crew briefing, passenger PA, dispatch coordination, maintenance interface. One miscommunication can be catastrophic (Tenerife 1977). Strong communication prevents errors and maintains situational awareness.
KEY POINTS:
- ATC: precise, read-back confirming understanding
- CRM: assertive communication prevents crew errors
- Cabin crew: safety briefing, emergency coordination
- Passenger PA: reduces anxiety, builds confidence in crew
- Ground/maintenance: clear squawks prevent return-to-service errors
AVOID: Vague answer. Missing the safety-critical nature.

Q: What is a pilot's role during an emergency?
EXPECTED: Cover both procedural and leadership aspects. Procedurally: aviate, navigate, communicate (in that order). Use checklists, declare emergency if warranted, coordinate with ATC, brief cabin crew, notify dispatch. Leadership: maintain calm, make clear decisions, land as soon as practicable.
KEY POINTS:
- Aviate first: maintain aircraft control
- Navigate: know where you are and where you're going
- Communicate: ATC, cabin crew, dispatch
- Use emergency checklists in priority order
- Declare emergency (Mayday/Pan-Pan) when warranted
- Debrief crew and file safety report afterward
AVOID: Jumping to communication before aircraft control. Not mentioning checklists.

Q: Tell me about your journey through aviation / your experience as a pilot.
EXPECTED: Detailed "tell me about yourself" with aviation specifics. Walk chronologically: first interest, first lesson, ratings acquired (PPL, IFR, CPL, CFI, ATP), hours by category, aircraft flown, previous employers. The story should arc naturally to: "and that's why I'm here today."
KEY POINTS:
- Origin of interest (specific and vivid)
- Ratings progression with approximate hours/timeframes
- Aircraft types flown
- Previous employers and roles
- Specific formative experiences
- Natural conclusion: why this airline, why now
AVOID: Pure chronological list with no story. Overly long — keep to 2-3 minutes.

SECTION 2: COMPETENCY / BEHAVIORAL — TMAAT (33 questions)
================================================================================

Q: Tell me about a time you discovered a safety issue — what did you do?
EXPECTED: Use STAR framework. The story demonstrates: you noticed something (situational awareness), you took action (didn't dismiss it), you followed the correct reporting/resolution process (SOPs), and the outcome was positive for safety. Strong examples: maintenance discrepancy on preflight walk-around, crew member showing signs of fatigue, NOTAM conflict, weight and balance anomaly.
KEY POINTS:
- Specific situation with clear safety implication
- Your action was proactive (didn't wait to see if it became a problem)
- You followed proper channels (maintenance, dispatch, chief pilot)
- No schedule pressure overrode the safety concern
- Documentation and follow-through
AVOID: Story where you bent rules but outcome was fine. Story where you hesitated too long before acting.

Q: Tell me about a time you had to put safety above schedule / customer pressure.
EXPECTED: Classic airline interview story. Pressure could come from: dispatch, captain, ground crew, gate agent, passengers. Your response should be calm, data-driven, non-confrontational but firm. Outcome: delayed or cancelled flight that was ultimately the right call.
KEY POINTS:
- Specific pressure source (dispatch, captain, ops, passengers)
- Your reasoning was data-based (weather, MEL, weight, fuel)
- You communicated respectfully but firmly
- You involved appropriate parties (maintenance, dispatch, chief pilot if needed)
- The decision was validated by events or post-event review
AVOID: Story where you gave in and got away with it.

Q: Tell me about a time you refused to fly or pushed back on an unsafe request.
EXPECTED: Specific, clear example — not hypothetical. The request could come from a captain, dispatcher, or operations. Your response was direct and professional: stated your reason clearly, referenced the specific regulation or policy, and didn't back down. Shows you can be assertive without being aggressive.
KEY POINTS:
- Specific request and why it was unsafe (regulation/policy reference)
- Your response: calm, professional, but firm
- How the other party reacted and how you managed it
- Outcome: the flight was delayed/cancelled and that was the right outcome
- What you learned about standing your ground professionally
AVOID: Vague story. Story where you eventually went along with the unsafe request.

Q: Tell me about a time you made a mistake — what happened and what did you learn?
EXPECTED: Interviewers want: genuine ownership (no blame-shifting), clear explanation of what happened, immediate corrective action, and concrete lessons learned that changed your behavior. Mistakes acceptable: missed a callout, incomplete approach brief, communication error with ATC, miscalculated performance.
KEY POINTS:
- Own it fully — no blame-shifting to others or circumstances
- Describe the mistake clearly and honestly
- Immediate corrective action you took
- Long-term behavioral change resulting from the lesson
- Proportional response — shows professional maturity
AVOID: Blaming others or equipment. Using a regulatory violation. Minimizing the mistake.

Q: Tell me about a time you had to make a difficult decision under pressure.
EXPECTED: Classic judgment/decision-making question. Use a framework implicitly: gather facts, assess options, evaluate risks, decide, execute, check. Good examples: diversion decision with marginal fuel, go-around vs. continue in deteriorating weather, dispatch with complex MEL. Show external pressure did not compromise your decision process.
KEY POINTS:
- Clear description of the pressure (time, external actors, competing interests)
- Systematic decision process (gather facts, options, risks)
- The decision made and why
- How you communicated the decision to stakeholders
- Outcome and what you'd do the same/differently
AVOID: Story where you rushed and got lucky. Story without a clear decision process.

Q: Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a Captain or First Officer.
EXPECTED: Conflict should be professional, not personal. You addressed it using CRM principles: raised the concern assertively but respectfully, framed it around safety or procedure, listened to their perspective, reached a resolution. Do NOT use a story where you were clearly right and they were clearly wrong.
KEY POINTS:
- Professional disagreement about a procedure, routing, or approach strategy
- You raised it using graduated assertiveness (query → assert → challenge)
- You listened to their reasoning
- Resolution was collaborative or you escalated appropriately
- Debrief happened afterward; relationship was maintained
AVOID: Personal conflict. Story where you were obviously right and they obviously wrong. Making your crewmember look bad.

Q: Tell me about a time you had to be assertive with a more senior crew member.
EXPECTED: Core CRM skill. Show you understand the authority gradient and why passive behavior with an erring senior crew member is dangerous. Use graduated assertiveness: query, assert, challenge. The story should show you spoke up, it was received well or resolved safely, and you maintained a professional relationship.
KEY POINTS:
- Clear situation where inaction would have been dangerous
- Graduated assertiveness: started with a query, escalated as needed
- Professional tone — not accusatory, data-based
- Outcome: the error was caught or corrected
- Relationship with the senior crew member maintained
AVOID: Story where you aggressively challenged the captain. Story where you just went along with it.

Q: Tell me about a time you worked with a difficult crew member.
EXPECTED: "Difficult" can mean: poor CRM, fatigue-related errors, interpersonal friction, skill gap. Key is how you managed it: adapted your communication style, addressed the performance issue directly but respectfully, maintained crew safety and harmony. Did not ignore it or escalate unnecessarily.
KEY POINTS:
- Specific description of the difficulty (be professional, not gossipy)
- Your approach: direct communication, adapted style
- How you maintained safety and flight performance despite the friction
- How the situation resolved
- What you learned about managing crew dynamics
AVOID: Making the other crew member look terrible.

Q: Tell me about a time you failed as a leader.
EXPECTED: Genuine humility. Examples: misjudged crew briefing and left crew unprepared, failed to catch an error because you didn't create psychological safety, made a decision without consulting crew that you should have. Own the failure, describe the impact, explain what you changed.
KEY POINTS:
- Genuine failure of leadership, not a trivial misstep
- Honest assessment of your role in the failure
- Impact on the crew or operation
- Specific behavioral change made afterward
- Evidence the change took effect in subsequent leadership situations
AVOID: Fake failure that's actually a strength in disguise. Blaming your crew.

Q: Tell me about a time you successfully led a team.
EXPECTED: Concrete example with clear leadership behaviors: briefed the team, set expectations, leveraged individual strengths, communicated clearly under pressure, adjusted plan when needed, achieved the goal. Aviation example: successfully managing an abnormal or emergency with full crew coordination.
KEY POINTS:
- Clear leadership behaviors (not just "I was in charge")
- How you briefed and prepared the team
- A challenge during execution and how you responded
- The outcome and team response
- What you'd do the same or differently
AVOID: "I was the leader and things went well" with no specifics.

Q: Tell me about a time you provided exceptional customer service.
EXPECTED: Airlines are customer service companies. Examples: going above and beyond for a passenger in distress, creative solution to a disrupted passenger, calming PA during rough weather, connecting with passengers during a delay. Reflect genuine care, not just compliance with service standards.
KEY POINTS:
- Specific passenger or situation
- Action that went beyond minimum requirements
- The passenger's positive response
- Reflection on why customer service is integral to your professional identity
AVOID: Generic "I always smile and say hello" answer.

Q: Tell me about a time you turned a stressful situation into a positive experience.
EXPECTED: Southwest culture question (valid for all airlines). Stressful situation: severe weather delay, ground stop, mechanical issue. Your response: communicated transparently, found creative solutions, maintained positive energy, turned frustrated passengers into ambassadors. Shows emotional intelligence and leadership.
KEY POINTS:
- A specific stressful operational scenario
- Proactive communication with passengers/crew
- Creative or above-standard response
- Maintained positive professional energy
- The passenger/crew response was positive
AVOID: Minimizing the stress. Missing the emotional intelligence component.

Q: Tell me about a time you helped someone without being asked.
EXPECTED: Southwest culture question about servant's heart. You noticed a need and met it without expecting recognition. Shows empathy and proactive helpfulness. Aviation-related (helping junior crew member, assisting passenger without being directed) or life-related.
KEY POINTS:
- You noticed the need without it being flagged to you
- Action was genuinely helpful (not performative)
- The person benefited meaningfully
- No expectation of recognition
- Brief reflection on why this matters to you
AVOID: A contrived story. Anything that makes you look like you want credit.

Q: Tell me about a time you had to adapt to a last-minute change.

Q: Tell me about a time you dealt with extreme stress.

Approach briefing under pressure, completing a complex MEL task correctly, following weight and balance procedures despite rushing ground crew.

Q: Tell me about a time you made a tough decision despite pressure from others.

Q: Your captain descends below minimum vectoring altitude saying he "knows the terrain." What do you do?
EXPECTED: Classic authority gradient scenario. Use graduated assertiveness: query ("I show us below the MVA here — is that correct?"), assert if no response ("Captain, we need to climb — we're below safe altitude"), challenge if still no response ("I'm calling ATC for confirmation."). Reference chart data, not personal preference. The right action is clear — this is a life-safety issue, not a preference disagreement. Never silently comply.
KEY POINTS:
- Immediate query using data: "I show us at [altitude], MVA is [altitude] — should we check this?"
- Graduated escalation if no acknowledgment
- Reference specific chart or FMS data, not "I feel" language
- Involve ATC if captain doesn't respond appropriately
- Document afterward; possible ASAP report
AVOID: Silent compliance. Aggressive takeover without communication. Waiting too long to speak.

Q: Your captain consistently skips a checklist item. What do you do?
EXPECTED: First occurrence: politely draw attention ("I don't see us completing [item] — want me to call it out?"). Pattern: address it directly but respectfully, one-on-one before the flight. Not improving: document, consider ASAP/SMS report, involve chief pilot through proper channels. Never: ignore it, confront publicly, threaten immediately.
KEY POINTS:
- First occurrence: gentle in-cockpit reminder
- Pattern: direct, private, respectful conversation with the captain
- Frame it around SOP and safety, not personal criticism
- If continues: document and use proper reporting channels (ASAP, SMS)
- Chief pilot notification if safety is genuinely at risk
AVOID: Ignoring it. Public confrontation. Immediately going over the captain's head without direct communication first.

Q: At 500 feet, unstabilized, the captain is continuing the approach. What do you do?
EXPECTED: Call it out immediately per SOPs: "Unstabilized — go around." If no response: "Going around" — initiate the go-around yourself. You have both the authority and the responsibility to call a go-around as the PM or as PF. Stabilized approach criteria exist precisely for this situation. Document and debrief afterward calmly.
KEY POINTS:
- Immediate callout: "Unstabilized" per SOP
- If no response or acknowledgment: call the go-around
- Execute the go-around — do not continue an unstabilized approach
- Debrief afterward, maintain professional relationship
- ASAP report if the captain actively refused a go-around call
AVOID: Silently hoping the approach works out. Waiting for a lower altitude to speak up. Calling go-around and then not executing it.

Q: On final, weather is now below minimums and the captain wants to continue. What do you do?
EXPECTED: This is a legal and safety violation if continued. Clearly state the minimums: "Our minimums are [DA/RVR]. Current ATIS shows [conditions] — we're not legal to continue. We need to go missed." If captain insists: "I cannot continue this approach below minimums — I'm going to initiate the missed approach." This is not a judgment call — it's a regulation.
KEY POINTS:
- Reference the specific minimums and current conditions by number
- State clearly that it's not legal to continue
- Initiate missed approach if captain doesn't respond
- Communicate with ATC
- Document thoroughly; possible ASAP report or FAA report if captain forced a below-minimums approach
AVOID: Continuing the approach and hoping. Vague language without numbers. Not acting as the final check on a regulatory violation.

Q: Your captain appears intoxicated before the flight. What do you do?
EXPECTED: Bright-line issue — DO NOT FLY. Address privately and respectfully: "I'm concerned about how you're doing — I don't think we should fly today." Contact operations/chief pilot immediately. Document everything. Your career concern is secondary to safety. The regulation (FAR 91.17) is clear: no alcohol within 8 hours, no flying with BAC above 0.04%.
KEY POINTS:
- DO NOT fly under any circumstances
- Address privately first: respectful but direct
- Immediately contact operations or chief pilot
- Document all observations with specifics (time, behavior)
- Support will be available — this is a protected reporting category
- Never let schedule pressure or personal relationships override this call
AVOID: Flying and hoping for the best. Confronting the captain publicly. Waiting to see if it gets better.

Q: Your captain becomes incapacitated mid-flight. What do you do?
EXPECTED: Take the controls immediately if you don't already have them. Secure the incapacitated pilot (seatbelt, move if causing control interference). Declare Mayday with ATC. Brief cabin crew (send up senior FA if possible). Divert to nearest suitable airport. Get assistance from any deadheading pilot if available. Follow incapacitation checklist. This is a practiced response — not improvisation.
KEY POINTS:
- Take the controls immediately
- Secure the incapacitated pilot (seatbelt)
- Declare MAYDAY with ATC: "MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY — pilot incapacitation, request immediate vectors to nearest suitable airport"
- Brief senior cabin crew, request they come to flight deck if needed
- Request deadheading pilot assistance if available
- Follow checklist; divert to nearest suitable
AVOID: Panicking or delaying. Trying to revive the pilot before securing the aircraft. Not declaring the emergency.

Q: You're 45 minutes out, weather has deteriorated, alternate is marginal, fuel is at minimum. What do you do?
EXPECTED: Use FORDEC or TDODAR to work through this systematically. First: declare minimum fuel to ATC (priority handling, not yet emergency). Request direct routing to destination. Assess: if destination is still achievable, continue; if not, identify best alternate with fuel. Declare fuel MAYDAY if fuel genuinely emergency-level. Earlier action is always better — declare EARLY. Never let fuel become an unmanaged emergency.
KEY POINTS:
- Declare "MINIMUM FUEL" to ATC immediately (not an emergency call yet — just a heads-up)
- Request direct routing to save fuel
- Reassess destination viability with current information
- If destination not viable: declare MAYDAY fuel emergency, divert to best alternate with clear weather
- Coordinate with dispatch for updated destination weather
- Better to declare early — documentation protects you
AVOID: Hoping for the best without declaring minimum fuel. Waiting until actual emergency. Not using ATC priority handling.

Q: Your FO and a flight attendant are arguing before boarding. What do you do?
EXPECTED: Separate them immediately, address each privately. As captain: you have authority and responsibility for crew harmony and fitness for duty. Assess whether either is emotionally unfit to fly. Set clear professional expectations. Delay departure if necessary to resolve the crew dynamics issue. Document; debrief after flight. Crew conflict before departure is a TEM (Threat and Error Management) issue.
KEY POINTS:
- Separate them immediately
- Address each privately — hear both perspectives
- Set clear professional expectations as captain
- Assess fitness for duty (emotional state affecting performance)
- Delay departure if needed to resolve
- Document and debrief after flight
AVOID: Ignoring it. Trying to resolve it in front of passengers. Taking sides publicly.

Q: You're on day 4 of a trip, exhausted but legal; captain says push through. What do you do?
EXPECTED: You have the authority and obligation to call fatigue. Legal does not mean fit. "I need to be honest — I'm not feeling fit to fly safely today." Fatigue calls are protected under most airline policies and Part 117 intent. Document the fatigue. Discuss with captain and operations. The captain cannot override your fatigue call — it's your professional and regulatory obligation.
KEY POINTS:
- Legal to fly does not mean fit to fly
- You have the authority to call fatigue — it is protected
- Communicate clearly: "I don't feel I'm fit to fly safely"
- Document the fatigue call
- Discuss options with operations: rest period, replacement crew
- The captain's pressure does not override your fitness assessment
AVOID: Flying when genuinely impaired. Using fatigue as an excuse when you're just tired of the trip. Not documenting.

Q: Thunderstorms building at destination; ATC offers vector to final but FO flags windshear. What do you do?
EXPECTED: The FO's input is valuable — this is CRM working correctly. Assess all available information: airborne weather radar, PIREPS, ATIS, company weather updates. If windshear is a credible threat, do not continue the approach. Discuss as crew, make a decision together. Options: hold and wait for conditions to improve, divert to alternate, or request a different approach. Safety over schedule — always.
KEY POINTS:
- Acknowledge and value the FO's input ("Good callout — let's assess")
- Check all available weather sources: radar, ATIS, PIREPS, company weather
- Discuss options as a crew: hold, divert, different approach
- Do not continue into known windshear
- Coordinate with dispatch and ATC
- Document the decision
AVOID: Dismissing the FO's concern. Continuing without assessing the threat. Being pressured by ATC convenience.

Q: You find a discrepancy not in the logbook, mentioned verbally by the previous crew. What do you do?
EXPECTED: A verbal report of a discrepancy is not the same as a properly documented and released squawk. You cannot accept the aircraft as airworthy based on a verbal report. Contact maintenance to formally document, evaluate, and either repair or defer the item under the MEL. Verbal-only maintenance status is not acceptable for Part 121 operations.
KEY POINTS:
- Verbal report does not substitute for proper documentation
- Contact maintenance immediately
- Maintenance must evaluate and either repair or formally defer under MEL
- You cannot dispatch without formal maintenance release
- Document the communication in writing
- Do not pressure maintenance for a quick verbal approval
AVOID: Accepting the aircraft based on the verbal report. Dispatching without a maintenance release. Documenting the verbal report and considering it resolved.

Q: Ground crew is rushing a turn but weight and balance isn't complete. What do you do?
EXPECTED: Weight and balance is a legal requirement for departure — it cannot be skipped. Push back and hold. "We're not ready to push — W&B is not complete." You have the authority to hold the departure. On-time performance does not override regulatory compliance or safety. If pressure continues, contact ops control or your chief pilot.
KEY POINTS:
- Weight and balance must be complete before pushback — it's a legal requirement
- Push back and hold until W&B is complete
- Communicate clearly: "We will not push until weight and balance is complete and correct"
- If needed, contact ops control
- Document the delay and the reason
- On-time performance never overrides this requirement
AVOID: Accepting estimated or informal W&B. Pushing back and hoping the formal W&B matches. Being rushed into departure before mandatory paperwork is done.

Q: Dispatch pressures you to depart with a questionable MEL item. What do you do?
EXPECTED: The Captain is the final authority on whether to depart. A questionable MEL item must be understood and accepted, not just rubber-stamped. Review the MEL entry carefully: is the deferral correctly documented? Are the required inspection/action items complete? If you're not comfortable: consult maintenance directly. Dispatch advises; the captain decides. You cannot be forced to depart with an item you're not satisfied with.
KEY POINTS:
- Review the MEL entry thoroughly
- Confirm the required actions/placard items are complete
- Consult maintenance directly if uncertain
- Dispatch advises — captain decides; you cannot be overridden
- Communicate your reasoning clearly
- If still not comfortable: document and do not depart
AVOID: Rubber-stamping the MEL item because dispatch says it's fine. Departing with an item you don't understand. Being bullied into a departure.

Q: ATC clears you to an altitude that would cause a TCAS conflict. What do you do?
EXPECTED: If you receive a TCAS RA (Resolution Advisory): FOLLOW THE RA, NOT ATC. This is the rule. Advise ATC you're "Unable, TCAS RA." ATC expects this response — the system is designed this way. If it's a potential TCAS advisory (TA only): coordinate with ATC. The RA is the safety net; it overrides ATC instructions during a TCAS event.
KEY POINTS:
- If RA (Resolution Advisory) is issued: follow the RA immediately
- Advise ATC: "Unable, TCAS Resolution Advisory"
- The RA overrides ATC instructions during a TCAS event
- Once clear of conflict: return to ATC cleared altitude and advise
- Document the event; file ASAP report
AVOID: Following ATC instead of the RA. Ignoring the RA. Not advising ATC of the deviation.

Q: Your FO is flying and appears fatigued, making errors. What do you do?
EXPECTED: As PIC, you are responsible for the safety of the flight. Address the fatigue directly: "I'm noticing some errors — how are you feeling? Are you fit to continue?" You may need to take the controls and have the FO in a monitoring role only. Consider declaring a reduced-crew situation to ATC/dispatch if severe. The FO's fatigue is your problem to manage.
KEY POINTS:
- Address it directly and privately: "Are you feeling okay? I'm noticing some errors."
- You can take the controls if necessary
- Monitor the FO closely for further degradation
- Consider divert or operations notification if fatigue is severe
- Document the situation
AVOID: Ignoring the errors. Waiting for a bigger error to occur. Embarrassing the FO in front of cabin crew or ATC.

Q: A passenger is combative and needs to be removed. What do you do?
EXPECTED: This is primarily a cabin crew management issue, but as captain you have final authority. Support the flight attendants' assessment. Coordinate with ground personnel and gate agents. Use law enforcement if necessary — do not put cabin crew at risk. Document thoroughly. Your priority: safety of all passengers and crew, not confrontation management.
KEY POINTS:
- Support cabin crew's assessment — they are trained for this
- Communicate with gate agents/ground operations to arrange removal
- Contact law enforcement if needed
- As PIC, you make the final call on whether the passenger can remain aboard
- Document everything thoroughly
- Do not depart with an unmanaged combative passenger
AVOID: Confronting the passenger yourself. Departing while the situation is unresolved. Overriding cabin crew without good reason.

Q: Maintenance says the aircraft is airworthy but you're not satisfied. What do you do?
EXPECTED: The captain has the legal authority (and obligation) to determine the aircraft is airworthy before departure. If you're not satisfied: ask more questions, request a different maintenance technician, ask for the maintenance supervisor. You do not have to depart if you are not satisfied. Dispatch pressure and maintenance sign-off do not override your judgment. Document your concerns.
KEY POINTS:
- You have the right and obligation to be satisfied with airworthiness
- Ask more specific questions: "Can you show me the work order? Where is the defect documented?"
- Request maintenance supervisor if needed
- You do not depart if you are not comfortable
- Document your concerns in writing
- This is protected under FAR — you cannot be punished for a legitimate airworthiness concern
AVOID: Departing because maintenance said it was fine when you're not satisfied. Being bullied by schedule pressure. Not documenting your concerns.

Q: Your FO strongly disagrees with your routing decision mid-flight. How do you handle it?
EXPECTED: CRM requires taking crew input seriously. Ask the FO to explain their reasoning. If they have good information or a valid safety concern, adjust the decision. If you've considered their input and maintain your decision: explain your reasoning clearly and proceed. The captain makes the final call, but that call should be informed by the FO's input. Acknowledge the disagreement professionally.
KEY POINTS:
- "Tell me your concern — I'm listening"
- Genuinely consider the FO's argument
- If the FO is right: change the decision and acknowledge it
- If you maintain your decision: explain your reasoning clearly
- Make the decision as a team if time allows; execute as captain
- Debrief after: "Let's talk about that disagreement — both perspectives were valuable"
AVOID: Dismissing the FO without hearing them. Changing your decision just to avoid conflict. Making the FO feel their input doesn't matter.

Q: You see another aircraft on a collision course and ATC hasn't called the traffic. What do you do?
EXPECTED: Your first obligation is collision avoidance. If you see conflicting traffic, you are authorized to take evasive action. Advise ATC immediately: "[Callsign], traffic alert, taking evasive action." Use TCAS guidance if issued. After the maneuver: advise ATC of your position and deviation. Do not wait for ATC to call traffic if you can see the conflict visually.
KEY POINTS:
- Take evasive action if necessary for immediate safety
- Advise ATC: "Traffic alert, taking evasive action"
- Use TCAS if available and RA is issued
- After maneuver: advise ATC of position, altitude, and return to clearance
- Document the event; file ASAP and near-miss reports
AVOID: Waiting for ATC to call the traffic when you can see it. Not advising ATC of your deviation. Ignoring the traffic because it's ATC's job to call it.

Q: Engine failure on takeoff at V1 — walk me through your actions.
EXPECTED: At V1: continue the takeoff (abort is no longer the correct choice). Rotate at VR to achieve V2. Don't chase V2 — rotate smoothly to the V2 attitude. Gear up on positive rate. At acceleration altitude: identify, verify, feather/shut down failed engine (if applicable). Run the engine failure checklist. Coordinate with ATC for return. Passenger briefing via cabin crew. Land at nearest suitable. This is a trained emergency.
KEY POINTS:
- At V1 and beyond: continue the takeoff
- Rotate smoothly at VR to initial climb attitude targeting V2
- Positive rate: gear up
- Don't chase airspeed — fly the attitude
- Identify and confirm the failed engine before any shutdown action
- Contact ATC, declare emergency if needed
- Execute the QRH/checklist for the specific engine failure type
AVOID: Aborting above V1. Chasing airspeed rather than flying attitude. Rushing to shut down an engine without verification.

Q: Rapid decompression at FL350 — what are your immediate actions?
EXPECTED: Immediate recall items (from memory): Don oxygen masks immediately (within seconds — time of useful consciousness at FL350 is approximately 15-30 seconds). Establish communications between crew with masks on. Begin emergency descent immediately (deploy speed brakes, reduce power, descend at maximum rate to 10,000 ft or MEA, whichever is higher). Squawk 7700. ATC call. Then: assess situation, coordinate with cabin crew (oxygen deployment confirmation), divert to nearest suitable.
KEY POINTS:
- Crew oxygen masks ON immediately (do not delay)
- Emergency descent: full speedbrakes, reduce power, descent attitude — target 10,000 ft or MEA
- Squawk 7700
- ATC: "MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY — [callsign], rapid decompression, emergency descent to [altitude]"
- Confirm passenger oxygen deployment with cabin crew
- Divert to nearest suitable airport
AVOID: Trying to communicate without masks on first. Delaying the descent. Forgetting to squawk emergency.

Q: Windshear warning on approach — what do you do?
EXPECTED: Execute the windshear escape maneuver immediately: TOGA power, rotate to initial climb attitude (windshear escape pitch attitude per the aircraft type, typically 15-20 degrees nose-up), do NOT chase the glidepath. Call "Windshear — go around" and advise ATC. Do not attempt to re-land until conditions are confirmed clear. File a PIREP. Do not let a smooth approach earlier in the flight give you false confidence about the conditions at the threshold.
KEY POINTS:
- TOGA power immediately
- Rotate to windshear escape attitude (aircraft-specific, typically 15-20 degrees)
- Do NOT chase the glidepath — fly the escape attitude
- Advise ATC and declare emergency if needed
- Do not re-attempt the approach until conditions confirmed safe
- File PIREP for other crews
AVOID: Chasing the glidepath during a microburst (the classic accident scenario). Continuing the approach. Reducing power during the escape.

Q: You're cleared for an approach that would be below minimums. What do you do?
EXPECTED: "Unable" — advise ATC that you cannot accept the clearance as current conditions are below minimums for the approach. Request a higher minimum approach, a hold until conditions improve, or divert to alternate. You are not legal to begin an approach if the current reported conditions are below minimums for that approach. ATC does not override minimums.
KEY POINTS:
- "Unable — current conditions are below minimums for this approach"
- Request a higher minimum approach alternative if available
- Request a hold if conditions are improving
- Advise diversion if no suitable approach is available
- Document the exchange
AVOID: Accepting the clearance and hoping conditions improve during the approach. Arguing with ATC. Continuing below minimums without required visual references.

Q: Your captain breaks a regulation or does something unsafe — what do you do?
EXPECTED: Use graduated assertiveness: query (confirm it happened — could be a misunderstanding), assert (clearly state the concern using data/regulation), challenge if necessary. After the flight: document and file appropriate safety/compliance reports (ASAP, SMS, or regulatory report depending on severity). Your obligation to safety and compliance does not end after the flight.
KEY POINTS:
- Query first: confirm what you observed; it might be a misunderstanding
- Assert: "I need to flag this — we just [action] which I believe is [regulation ref]"
- Challenge if necessary: "I can't continue without resolving this"
- After flight: document and file ASAP/SMS report regardless of how captain reacted
- For serious violations: contact chief pilot or flight standards
AVOID: Ignoring it because it's the captain. Immediately threatening regulatory action without direct communication. Not documenting.

Q: On the takeoff roll near V1, you get a non-critical warning light. What do you do?
EXPECTED: This is the classic "V1 trap" scenario. The answer depends entirely on the type of warning and whether it requires an abort. If you are approaching V1 and the light is non-critical (and does not require an immediate stop): continue the takeoff and address after airborne. If the light requires an abort per QRH and you have sufficient runway (below V1): reject the takeoff. The key: do not reject above V1 for a non-critical item. Know your reject vs. continue criteria.
KEY POINTS:
- Below V1 with a reject-required item and sufficient runway: REJECT
- Near V1 with non-critical item: CONTINUE — the runway ahead is less than the runway required to stop
- Above V1: continue the takeoff regardless — stopping is more dangerous
- After airborne: address the warning per QRH
- The PM should be monitoring and calling out V-speeds
AVOID: Rejecting above V1 for a non-critical warning. Not knowing your specific aircraft's reject/continue criteria.

Q: A passenger confronts you for going around. How do you handle it?
EXPECTED: Calm, confident, factual explanation without being defensive or apologetic. The go-around was the right call — explain briefly why it was necessary for safety. Acknowledge the passenger's frustration with empathy. Reinforce that safety is always the priority. Do not over-explain or be defensive. A brief explanation with genuine confidence is far more reassuring than a lengthy justification.
KEY POINTS:
- Empathy: "I completely understand that was unexpected and I appreciate your patience"
- Brief factual explanation: "We were not in a safe position to land, so the safest option was to go around"
- Confidence: no apology for the correct safety decision
- No technical jargon the passenger won't understand
- Offer to answer questions if appropriate
AVOID: Being defensive or apologetic about a correct safety decision. Over-explaining with technical details. Getting into a debate about whether it was necessary.

Q: Holding because destination is closed; alternate is marginal, another is 30 min away but clear. What do you do?
EXPECTED: Use FORDEC. Time: how long can you hold before fuel forces a decision? Options: continue holding (destination might reopen), divert to marginal alternate, divert to clear alternate (30 min away). Risk: marginal alternate could go below minimums; clear alternate 30 min away is more fuel. Decision: likely divert to clear alternate with enough fuel margin rather than risk a marginal alternate. Coordinate with dispatch.
KEY POINTS:
- Calculate fuel state: how long can you hold vs. fuel required for each option?
- Coordinate with dispatch for destination weather updates
- Assess marginal alternate: is it improving or deteriorating?
- 30-min alternate with clear weather is the lower-risk option if fuel allows
- Declare minimum fuel if holding is extending beyond normal
- Make the decision early — don't let the situation become an emergency
AVOID: Holding until fuel forces a bad decision. Going to the marginal alternate without a fuel contingency. Not coordinating with dispatch.

Q: What measures do you take when operating in inclement weather?
EXPECTED: A systematic, multi-phase answer. Pre-flight: thorough weather brief (ATIS, METARs, TAFs, PIREPs, SIGMETs, radar), alternate filed with solid weather, fuel contingency added, personal minimums set. In flight: continuous weather monitoring, dispatch coordination for updates, ATC for deviations, fuel managed conservatively. On approach: go-around is always an option; never self-pressure to continue below minimums. Crew briefing on threats and backup plans.
KEY POINTS:
- Pre-flight: comprehensive weather brief including all products (METAR, TAF, SIGMET, PIREP, radar)
- File with a solid alternate; add contingency fuel
- Crew briefing: specific weather threats, go-around criteria explicit
- In flight: continuous monitoring; dispatch and ATC communication
- Approach: stabilized approach criteria enforced; go-around at any doubt
- Personal minimums above regulatory minimums
AVOID: "I just check the ATIS." Missing the systematic multi-source weather assessment. Not mentioning go-around as the default safety valve.

Q: What is the maximum airspeed in Class C airspace?
EXPECTED: 200 knots indicated airspeed (KIAS) within 4 nautical miles of the primary airport of a Class C airspace area, below 2,500 feet AGL above the airport elevation. This applies to the airspace around the primary airport, not all of Class C. Note: within 4 NM of a Class B airport below 10,000 MSL, the speed limit is also 200 KIAS.
KEY POINTS:
- 200 KIAS in Class C airspace
- Within 4 NM of primary Class C airport, below 2,500 AGL
- Also 200 KIAS within 4 NM of Class B primary airport
- 250 KIAS below 10,000 MSL everywhere else (unless prohibited by airspace)
AVOID: Confusing Class B and Class C limits. Forgetting the altitude and distance qualifiers.

Q: At what altitude does the 250-knot speed restriction end?
EXPECTED: The 250-knot speed restriction applies below 10,000 feet MSL. At and above 10,000 feet MSL, there is no FAR-mandated upper speed limit (other than VMO/MMO). Note: the restriction is below 10,000 feet MSL, not AGL. So a mountainous area where the terrain is at 9,000 feet still requires 250 knots or less until 10,000 MSL.
KEY POINTS:
- 250 KIAS below 10,000 feet MSL
- At 10,000 MSL and above: no FAR speed limit (VMO/MMO apply)
- MSL, not AGL
- Class B/C inner ring has the 200-knot restriction superseding this
AVOID: Saying AGL instead of MSL. Forgetting the Class B/C inner ring lower limits.

Q: What is required to descend below DA/DH on an ILS (FAR 91.175)?
EXPECTED: Two things must both be true to descend below DA/DH: (1) The required flight visibility must exist (actual flight visibility at or above the approach minimums), AND (2) one of the ten visual references specified in FAR 91.175(c)(3) must be clearly visible and identifiable. Additionally, the aircraft must be in a position from which a normal approach and landing can be made using normal maneuvers. If either condition is not met, execute the missed approach.
KEY POINTS:
- Required flight visibility must exist
- At least ONE of the FAR 91.175 visual references must be visible (approach lights, threshold, touchdown zone, runway markings, runway lights, REIL, VASI/PAPI, touchdown zone lights, runway centerline lights, or other markings/lights identifying the runway)
- Must be in position for a normal approach using normal maneuvers
- All three conditions must be met simultaneously
AVOID: Thinking RVR and flight visibility are the same thing. Forgetting the "normal position" requirement.

Q: Can you depart under Part 121 when weather is below landing minimums?
EXPECTED: Yes, you CAN depart under Part 121 even when weather is below landing minimums at the departure airport — provided you have filed an alternate airport that meets alternate weather requirements. This is because you are departing with the intention to return to land at a different airport. Note: takeoff minimums still apply (you must be able to depart safely). Also, if the destination has weather below minimums, you must have an alternate.
KEY POINTS:
- Yes, departure is permitted even if below landing minimums
- Must file an alternate airport that meets alternate weather requirements
- Takeoff minimums still apply for departure
- Destination below minimums also requires an alternate
AVOID: Saying you cannot depart. Confusing takeoff minimums with landing minimums.

Q: When is a position report to ATC required on an IFR flight in VFR conditions? (MARVELOUS CVFR 500)
EXPECTED: MARVELOUS CVFR 500 is the mnemonic for mandatory position reports on an IFR clearance in VFR conditions (non-radar): M=Missed approach; A=Airspeed change (±5% or 10 kts); R=Reaching a holding fix; V=VFR on top; E=Leaving assigned altitude; L=Leaving holding fix; O=Outer marker inbound (non-radar); U=Unforecast weather; S=Safety of flight concern; C=Compulsory reporting points; VFR=Entering/leaving VFR-on-top; F=FAF inbound (non-radar); R=Radio/nav failure; 500=Altitude deviation of 300+ feet.
KEY POINTS:
- MARVELOUS CVFR 500 mnemonic covers all required reports
- Key ones: compulsory points, leaving/reaching altitudes, holding, missed, safety
- In radar environment: fewer required (ATC has your position)
- In non-radar environment: more frequent reports required
AVOID: Confusing non-radar with radar environment requirements.

Q: What is an LDA approach?
EXPECTED: A Localizer-type Directional Aid (LDA) approach is a non-precision approach using a localizer-type signal that is offset from the runway centerline (typically by 3-29 degrees). It provides lateral guidance only (no glideslope). Used when the runway alignment prevents a straight-in ILS — common at airports surrounded by terrain or obstacles. Has an MDA, not a DA, unless a glideslope supplement is added (called an LDA with glideslope).
KEY POINTS:
- Uses ILS localizer equipment but offset from runway centerline
- Non-precision: no glideslope (lateral guidance only)
- Has MDA, not DA
- Used when runway alignment prevents a straight-in ILS
- Some have glideslope supplement: LDA with glideslope
AVOID: Saying it has a glideslope. Confusing with SDF (Simplified Directional Facility).

Q: What is the difference between a Category I, II, and III ILS?
EXPECTED: Cat I: DH 200 ft, RVR 1800 ft (or 1/2 SM visibility). Requires standard ILS approach training. Cat II: DH 100 ft, RVR 1200 ft. Requires special Cat II authorization, training, and airport certification. Cat IIIa: DH less than 100 ft (or no DH), RVR 700 ft minimum. Cat IIIb: DH less than 50 ft (or no DH), RVR 150-700 ft. Cat IIIc: No DH, no RVR limit (full autoland in zero visibility — rare, special certification).
KEY POINTS:
- Cat I: DH 200 ft, RVR 1800 ft
- Cat II: DH 100 ft, RVR 1200 ft
- Cat IIIa: DH <100 ft, RVR ≥700 ft
- Cat IIIb: DH <50 ft, RVR 150-700 ft
- Cat IIIc: No DH, no RVR (rarely used)
- Each higher category requires additional training, equipment, and airport certification
AVOID: Mixing up the DH/RVR numbers. Forgetting that higher categories require special authorizations.

Q: What is RNAV and what is RNP?
EXPECTED: RNAV (Area Navigation) allows aircraft to fly any desired course within the coverage of ground-based or space-based navigation aids, or within the limits of self-contained aids. Aircraft can fly point-to-point without overflying ground stations. RNP (Required Navigation Performance) adds an onboard monitoring and alerting capability — the aircraft must be able to confirm it is operating within a specified accuracy (e.g., RNP 0.1 means within 0.1 NM 95% of the time, with alerting if it exceeds 2x that). RNP enables curved approaches into terrain-challenged airports.
KEY POINTS:
- RNAV: area navigation using GPS/FMS, not tied to ground navaids
- RNP: RNAV plus onboard performance monitoring and alerting
- RNP value: the required accuracy (e.g., RNP 0.1 = within 0.1 NM)
- RNP APCH enables precision-like curved approaches
- Modern airliners with FMS and GPS are RNP-capable (subject to authorization)
AVOID: Treating RNP and RNAV as the same thing.

Q: What is ETOPS and what does ETOPS 180 mean?
EXPECTED: ETOPS (Extended-range Twin-engine Operational Performance Standards, formerly Extended Twin Operations). Allows twin-engine airliners to fly routes that take them more than 60 minutes from a diversion airport. ETOPS 180 means the aircraft can operate on routes where the maximum diversion time to an alternate is 180 minutes at single-engine cruise speed. Requires specific maintenance programs, crew training, dispatch requirements, and spare systems. Allows twin-engine aircraft (B777, B787, A330) to operate transoceanic routes.
KEY POINTS:
- Allows twins to fly routes >60 minutes from a diversion airport
- ETOPS 180: diversion time up to 180 minutes at single-engine cruise speed
- Requires specific maintenance, training, dispatch standards
- Enables twin-engine transocean/polar operations
- Common aircraft: B777, B787, A330, A350
AVOID: Confusing the time limit (minutes at single-engine cruise speed, not normal cruise speed).

Q: What are the Part 117 flight/duty time and rest requirements?
EXPECTED: Part 117 applies to Part 121 operations. Flight Duty Period (FDP): limits vary by number of flight segments and report time relative to WOCL (Window of Circadian Low, 0200-0559 local). Typical maximum FDP: 9-14 hours depending on conditions. Max flight time: 8-9 hours in a single FDP; 100 hours in 672 hours (28 days); 1,000 hours in 365 days. Minimum rest before FDP: 10 hours with at least 8 hours uninterrupted sleep opportunity. Cumulative limits also apply.
KEY POINTS:
- FDP limit: 9-14 hours depending on start time and number of segments
- WOCL (0200-0559 local): reduces FDP limits
- Flight time max: 8-9 hrs per FDP; 100 hrs in 28 days; 1,000 hrs per year
- Minimum rest: 10 hours (8 hours uninterrupted sleep opportunity)
- Acclimation requirements for operations crossing many time zones
AVOID: Confusing FDP (flight duty period) with flight time. Forgetting the WOCL reduction.

Q: What is the difference between Part 91, Part 121, and Part 135?
EXPECTED: Part 91: General operating rules applying to all civil aircraft operations. No commercial compensation. Applies to general aviation and corporate flight departments. Part 121: Domestic and international scheduled air carrier operations (major/regional airlines). Strictest standards for aircraft, crew, training, dispatch, maintenance. Part 135: On-demand and commuter operations (charter, air taxi). Less restrictive than 121 but more than 91. Key difference: 121 requires a dispatch system (captain and dispatcher share authority); 135 has more captain-centric decision making; 91 is entirely PIC authority.
KEY POINTS:
- Part 91: general aviation rules, no fare for hire, PIC sole authority
- Part 121: air carrier (airlines), scheduled or non-scheduled, strictest standards
- Part 135: on-demand/charter/commuter, intermediate standards
- 121 unique: dual authority (captain + dispatcher); stringent rest/duty rules
- 135: some 121-like requirements but more flexibility
AVOID: Saying Part 135 is for "smaller aircraft" — it's an operational distinction, not a size distinction.

Q: What are your currency requirements?
EXPECTED: For airline pilots under Part 121: biennial flight review is superseded by 121 proficiency checks (every 6 months for line checks, annual simulator). IFR currency: 6 approaches in 6 months within the IFR environment (but Part 121 proficiency checks keep you current). Instrument currency requirement for Part 91 ops: 6 approaches, holds, intercepting/tracking in 6 months. PIC currency for a specific aircraft type: requires type rating checkride. Recurrent training: 9-month rolling period for most 121 carriers.
KEY POINTS:
- Part 121 operators: 6-month proficiency checks replace most currency requirements
- IFR currency: 6 approaches + holds + tracking in 6 months (Part 91 standard)
- Type rating required to act as PIC in specific aircraft
- Class I medical: required for ATP/airline operations, every 6 months under age 40, annually 40+
- Annual flight review superseded by 121/135 proficiency checks
AVOID: Giving only general aviation currency rules to an airline question.

Q: What is a Minimum Equipment List (MEL)?
EXPECTED: An MEL is a document approved by the FAA that allows an aircraft to be dispatched with certain inoperative equipment, under specified conditions and procedures. The MEL is derived from the Master Minimum Equipment List (MMEL) developed by the manufacturer and approved by the FAA. Operating with an inoperative item under the MEL requires: the MEL authorizes the item as deferrable, any required maintenance actions are performed, required operational procedures are followed, and the aircraft is placarded appropriately.
KEY POINTS:
- FAA-approved document allowing dispatch with inoperative equipment
- Derived from the MMEL (manufacturer's master list)
- To use an MEL item: item must be listed as deferrable; required (O) and (M) procedures must be performed
- Aircraft must be placarded
- Crew must be briefed on the MEL item and operational implications
AVOID: Thinking MEL means anything can be broken. Forgetting the required procedures and placarding.

Q: What are the alternate minimums for an IFR flight plan?
EXPECTED: Standard alternate minimums (FAR 91.169): For a precision approach at the alternate: 600-2. For a non-precision approach: 800-2. If the alternate has only circling approaches or no instrument approach: ceiling and visibility to ensure a visual approach is possible. Note: some airports in the A/FD have non-standard alternate minimums listed with an "A" symbol on approach plates. These must be used instead of the standard minimums.
KEY POINTS:
- Precision approach at alternate: 600 ft ceiling, 2 SM visibility
- Non-precision approach at alternate: 800 ft ceiling, 2 SM visibility
- Non-standard alternate minimums are published on approach plates with "A" symbol
- Some airports are designated NA (Not Authorized) as alternates under IFR
- 1-2-3 rule for needing an alternate: if within 1 hour of ETA, ceiling < 2,000 ft or visibility < 3 SM
AVOID: Forgetting the 1-2-3 rule for when an alternate is required.

Q: What are takeoff minimums under Part 121?
EXPECTED: Part 121 takeoff minimums are contained in FAR Part 121 Appendix and the airline's Ops Specs. Standard Part 121 takeoff minimums for a two-engine aircraft: RVR 1600 (or 1/4 SM). If published non-standard, the Ops Specs minimums must be used. A takeoff alternate is required if the departure airport weather is below landing minimums. A lower-than-standard takeoff RVR (down to 600 RVR for some operations) may be approved through Ops Specs.
KEY POINTS:
- Standard 2-engine: RVR 1600 (1/4 SM)
- Standard 3/4-engine: RVR 1200 (RVR table applies)
- Non-standard minimums published in airport/approach data
- Ops Specs may allow lower minimums (e.g., RVR 600 with specific equipment)
- Takeoff alternate required if departure weather below landing minimums
AVOID: Giving only general aviation takeoff minimums. Forgetting Ops Specs role.

Q: When is a takeoff alternate required?
EXPECTED: Under Part 121, a takeoff alternate must be designated if the weather at the departure airport is below landing minimums at the time of departure. The takeoff alternate must be within 1 hour (one engine inoperative) flying time from the departure airport. The takeoff alternate must meet alternate minimums at the estimated time of arrival.
KEY POINTS:
- Required when departure airport weather is below landing minimums
- Takeoff alternate must be within 1 hour at single-engine cruise speed
- Takeoff alternate must meet standard or non-standard alternate minimums at ETA
- Filed in the flight plan
AVOID: Confusing with the IFR en-route alternate.

Q: On an IFR flight in VFR conditions, who is responsible for separation?
EXPECTED: On an IFR flight in VFR conditions, the pilot is responsible for see-and-avoid with other traffic (like VFR). However, ATC is still responsible for providing IFR separation from other IFR traffic. The key: just because you're in VMC on an IFR clearance doesn't mean ATC fully handles separation — you still have to look out the window. Traffic advisories (VFR traffic) are issued on a workload-permitting basis by ATC.
KEY POINTS:
- ATC provides IFR separation from other IFR traffic
- Pilot is still responsible for see-and-avoid in VMC (FAR 91.113)
- ATC issues traffic advisories for VFR traffic workload permitting
- Not the same as being in IMC where ATC assumes all separation
AVOID: Saying ATC handles all separation. Not acknowledging the pilot's see-and-avoid responsibility.

Q: What are the three phases of a thunderstorm?
EXPECTED: Three phases: (1) Cumulus (developing) phase: strong updrafts, no precipitation reaching the ground, growing upward, lasts 10-15 minutes. (2) Mature phase: most hazardous — both updrafts and downdrafts exist simultaneously, heavy precipitation, lightning, hail, potential icing, possible tornadoes, microbursts. The top reaches the tropopause and spreads into an anvil shape. (3) Dissipating phase: predominantly downdrafts, precipitation decreasing, storm weakens. A squall line or mesoscale system can maintain itself indefinitely through new cell formation.
KEY POINTS:
- Cumulus: updrafts only, building
- Mature: both updrafts and downdrafts, most hazardous, all threats present
- Dissipating: mainly downdrafts, weakening
- Squall lines and MCS maintain themselves through new cell development
AVOID: Missing that the mature phase is the most dangerous. Forgetting to mention hail, lightning, and microburst potential.

Q: What is RVR and how does it affect Part 121 operations?
EXPECTED: RVR (Runway Visual Range) is the distance in feet that a pilot can see down the runway, measured by transmissometers installed along the runway. Used instead of visibility for approach and departure minimums at equipped airports. Part 121 ILS Cat I minimums: typically RVR 1800; Cat II: RVR 1200; Cat IIIa: RVR 700; Cat IIIb: RVR 150-700. RVR sensors are located at the touchdown zone (primary), midfield, and rollout point. Reported in hundreds of feet (e.g., RVR 2400).
KEY POINTS:
- Measured by transmissometers along the runway
- More precise than prevailing visibility for approach/departure operations
- Cat I: RVR 1800; Cat II: RVR 1200; Cat IIIa: RVR 700; Cat IIIb: RVR 150-700
- Three sensors: touchdown zone, midfield, rollout
- If RVR not available, convert to statute mile equivalent per chart notes
AVOID: Confusing RVR with prevailing visibility. Forgetting it's measured in feet (US) or meters (ICAO).

Q: How do you brief an approach plate?
EXPECTED: Use a systematic top-to-bottom and inside-out approach. Standard flow: (1) Airport identification and approach type; (2) Communication frequencies (ATIS, approach, tower, ground); (3) Plan view — overall picture, IAF, transitions, obstacles; (4) Profile view — altitude constraints, stepdown fixes, FAF; (5) Final approach course, DA/MDA, missed approach point; (6) Missed approach procedure; (7) Minimums — DA/MDA, visibility, alternate minimums; (8) Notes and special procedures; (9) Threats and TEM items.
KEY POINTS:
- Communications: ATIS, approach control, tower, ground
- Plan view: IAF, transition routes, holding patterns, obstacles
- Profile view: altitude steps, FAF, DA/MDA
- Final approach: course, fix, altimeter setting requirement
- Missed approach: complete procedure (heading, altitude, fix)
- Minimums: visibility and ceiling for all authorized approaches
- Notes: special procedures, non-standard items
AVOID: Missing the missed approach procedure. Skipping the notes section which often contains critical information.

Q: What is a stabilized approach — what are the criteria?
EXPECTED: Per IATA/FAA stabilized approach criteria, by 1,000 feet AGL in IMC (500 feet AGL in VMC) the following must be met: (1) Aircraft is on the correct lateral and vertical flight path (glidepath within 1 dot); (2) Airspeed within +/-10 kts of VREF (or within airline's specific limits); (3) Aircraft is in landing configuration (gear down, flaps in landing position); (4) Power is set for approach (above flight idle); (5) Checklist complete; (6) Sink rate does not exceed 1,000 fpm (1,300 fpm briefly acceptable); (7) No further large power changes required. If not stabilized at gate altitude: execute missed approach.
KEY POINTS:
- Gate altitude: 1,000 ft AGL in IMC; 500 ft AGL in VMC
- On correct flight path (glidepath within 1 dot)
- Correct airspeed (VREF +/- limits per airline SOPs)
- Correct configuration (gear, flaps)
- Power set (not at idle)
- Sink rate <1,000 fpm (<1,300 fpm brief exception)
- If NOT stabilized at gate altitude: go around — no exceptions
AVOID: Mentioning only speed. Missing that gate altitude is 1,000 ft IMC / 500 ft VMC.

Q: What is the 3:1 rule for descent planning?
EXPECTED: The 3:1 rule (also written as 3 NM per 1,000 ft) is a rule of thumb for planning a standard 3-degree descent path. For every 1,000 feet of altitude to lose, you need approximately 3 nautical miles of horizontal distance. To calculate your Top of Descent (TOD): multiply the altitude to lose (in thousands of feet) by 3, then add a fudge factor of 10-15 NM for deceleration. Example: Need to descend from FL350 to 5,000 ft (30,000 ft = 30 thousand feet): 30 x 3 = 90 NM + 15 NM buffer = start descent 105 NM from destination.
KEY POINTS:
- 3 NM per 1,000 feet of altitude to descend on a 3-degree path
- Formula: (altitude to lose in thousands) x 3 = approximate horizontal distance required
- Add ~10-15 NM for deceleration margin
- Also used to calculate required descent rate: groundspeed / 2 x 10 (in fpm)
- Example: 300 kts GS / 2 = 150, x 10 = 1,500 fpm to maintain 3 degrees
AVOID: Not having a practical formula memorized. Missing the deceleration buffer.

Q: What are the three types of structural icing?
EXPECTED: Three types of structural ice: (1) Rime ice — formed from small supercooled droplets that freeze instantly on contact; milky, opaque, brittle; builds on leading edges; most common in stratiform clouds; forms fastest at temperatures between 0 and -20°C. (2) Clear (glaze) ice — formed from larger droplets that spread before freezing; clear, hard, dense, heavy; most serious because it's hard to detect and difficult to remove; forms fastest near 0°C. (3) Mixed ice — a combination of rime and clear; can be the most serious combination for aircraft handling.
KEY POINTS:
- Rime: small droplets, milky/opaque, brittle, builds on leading edges
- Clear (glaze): larger droplets, transparent, hard, heavy, most dangerous
- Mixed: combination of both, can be most hazardous overall
- Temperature ranges: clear ice near 0°C; rime ice 0 to -20°C; below -20°C icing less likely
AVOID: Only knowing two types. Forgetting that clear ice is the most serious.

Q: What is a TAF and how do you use it for flight planning?
EXPECTED: TAF (Terminal Aerodrome Forecast) is a coded weather forecast for conditions within 5 statute miles of an airport, valid for 24 or 30 hours. Issued by NWS at select airports, updated every 6 hours. Used for: verifying destination weather will meet minimums at ETA, alternate airport weather assessment, deciding whether to add fuel. Time groups are in UTC (Zulu). TEMPO indicates temporary conditions; PROB30/40 indicates probability of conditions. FM indicates a permanent change from that time.
KEY POINTS:
- Forecast for conditions within 5 SM of the airport
- Valid for 24 or 30 hours; issued every 6 hours
- Used for destination, alternate, and en-route weather planning
- Key codes: FM (from), BECMG (becoming), TEMPO (temporary), PROB30/40 (probability)
- Decode in UTC; convert to local time for planning
AVOID: Confusing TAF with METAR (observation vs. forecast).

Q: What is a PIREP and when would you file one?
EXPECTED: PIREP (Pilot Report) is an observation by a pilot of actual meteorological conditions encountered in flight. Contains: aircraft type, location, altitude, time, conditions observed (turbulence intensity, icing type/intensity, clouds, visibility, wind). Filed with ATC by radio or ACARS. Should be filed for: any significant turbulence, icing, thunderstorm activity, unusual winds, windshear. Required filing: when encountering conditions not forecast (FAR 91.183 for IFR). Critical for other crews operating the same route.
KEY POINTS:
- Observed weather conditions from the cockpit
- Contents: aircraft type, location, altitude, time, condition encountered (intensity)
- Filed with ATC/FSDO by radio, ACARS, or telephone
- Should file for: significant turbulence, icing, unexpected conditions
- Benefits other crews and improves forecast accuracy
AVOID: Not knowing the components of a PIREP. Missing that filing can be required, not just optional.

Q: What is the difference between a SID and a STAR?
EXPECTED: SID (Standard Instrument Departure) is a published instrument departure procedure that provides obstacle clearance and routes departing aircraft from the airport to the en-route structure. STAR (Standard Terminal Arrival Route) is a published instrument arrival procedure that transitions aircraft from the en-route structure to the terminal area. Both are ATC tools for efficient traffic management and noise abatement. Both require pilot familiarization and briefing. Both can have altitude and speed restrictions that must be complied with unless amended by ATC.
KEY POINTS:
- SID: published departure procedure — airport to en-route structure
- STAR: published arrival procedure — en-route structure to terminal area/IAF
- Both have published altitude and speed restrictions
- Both require briefing as part of the approach/departure brief
- ATC can amend or cancel both with "Fly Heading" or "Expect" instructions
AVOID: Not knowing what SID and STAR stand for. Forgetting that restrictions must be complied with unless ATC amends.

Q: Explain the four forces of flight.
EXPECTED: Lift (acts perpendicular to the relative wind, upward in level flight) — generated primarily by the wings. Weight (gravity, acts downward through the center of gravity). Thrust (generated by engines, acts forward along the flight path). Drag (opposes motion through the air, acts rearward). In straight-and-level unaccelerated flight: Lift = Weight, Thrust = Drag. In a climb: Thrust > Drag. In an accelerating climb: all four forces are unbalanced. Lift is generated by angle of attack, airspeed, wing area, and air density — not exclusively the wing's shape (Bernoulli is part of the picture; Newton's third law also contributes).
KEY POINTS:
- Lift: perpendicular to relative wind, opposes weight
- Weight: downward through CG, opposes lift
- Thrust: forward, generated by engine(s), opposes drag
- Drag: rearward, opposes thrust and forward motion
- Straight-and-level: all four balanced (L=W, T=D)
AVOID: Saying lift is ONLY generated by wing shape (Bernoulli). Missing that all four must be balanced for level unaccelerated flight.

Q: What is the difference between parasite drag and induced drag?
EXPECTED: Parasite drag (also called profile drag) is the drag not related to lift generation. It includes form drag (shape of the aircraft), skin friction drag, and interference drag (where surfaces meet). Parasite drag INCREASES with the square of airspeed — double the speed, quadruple the parasite drag. Induced drag is a byproduct of lift generation. It results from the spanwise airflow around wingtips creating vortices, which tilt the lift vector rearward. Induced drag DECREASES as airspeed increases. At low speed: induced drag dominates. At high speed: parasite drag dominates. The intersection of the two curves is L/Dmax (best glide speed).
KEY POINTS:
- Parasite drag: form, skin friction, interference — increases with speed squared
- Induced drag: byproduct of lift generation, wingtip vortices — decreases with speed
- Total drag curve: U-shaped with minimum at L/Dmax (best glide speed)
- Low speed: induced drag dominant; high speed: parasite drag dominant
- L/Dmax = most efficient speed (best range or best glide)
AVOID: Getting the speed relationships backwards. Not knowing that they intersect at L/Dmax.

Q: What are winglets for?
EXPECTED: Winglets reduce induced drag by disrupting and containing the wingtip vortex (the spanwise flow from high-pressure below the wing to low-pressure above the wing around the tip). By effectively increasing the aspect ratio without increasing physical wingspan, winglets improve lift-to-drag ratio. This improves fuel efficiency (especially significant on long-haul routes) and reduces wake turbulence. Blended winglets (Boeing) and sharklets (Airbus) are common variants. They also provide structural benefits and reduce noise in some designs.
KEY POINTS:
- Reduce induced drag by disrupting wingtip vortices
- Effectively increase aspect ratio without adding wingspan
- Improve L/D ratio → better fuel efficiency
- Most effective on long-haul operations (fuel savings compound)
- Also reduce wake turbulence
AVOID: Saying winglets reduce parasite drag (they actually add a small amount). Missing the induced drag mechanism.

Q: What is coffin corner?
EXPECTED: Coffin corner refers to a narrow band of airspeeds at very high altitude where the stall speed and the maximum operating Mach number (MMO) converge. At high altitude, reduced air density means the aircraft must fly at a higher true airspeed to generate the same lift — this raises the stall TAS. Meanwhile, MMO (limited by compressibility effects) is relatively fixed in TAS terms. The two limits narrow, leaving the aircraft with very little speed margin. Flying too slow risks stall; flying too fast risks high-speed buffet and control problems. Occurs typically above 40,000-45,000 feet.
KEY POINTS:
- High altitude phenomenon where stall speed and MMO converge
- Stall speed increases with altitude (in TAS terms due to lower air density)
- MMO is relatively fixed in TAS
- The "corner" narrows until there's no safe speed margin
- Typical onset: 40,000-45,000 ft depending on aircraft weight and type
- Recovery: descend
AVOID: Not explaining WHY stall speed increases with altitude (density effect on TAS).

Q: What is Mach tuck / tuck-under?
EXPECTED: Mach tuck (also called tuck-under) is a nose-down pitching tendency that occurs at high Mach numbers. As the aircraft approaches the critical Mach number, a shock wave forms on the upper surface of the wing. The shock wave causes flow separation behind it and shifts the center of pressure rearward. This increases the downforce on the horizontal stabilizer, creating a nose-down pitching moment. As the nose drops, speed increases, the shock wave intensifies, and the pitching moment worsens — a potentially unrecoverable dive unless checked. Modern swept-wing aircraft with Mach trim systems automatically compensate for this tendency.
KEY POINTS:
- Occurs as aircraft approaches/exceeds critical Mach
- Shock wave formation causes rearward shift of center of pressure
- Increased tail-down force → nose pitches down
- Speed increases → condition worsens (divergent instability)
- Recovery: reduce thrust, extend speedbrakes, nose-up input
- Modern aircraft: Mach trim system automatically compensates
AVOID: Confusing Mach tuck with dutch roll. Missing the center-of-pressure shift mechanism.

Q: What is critical Mach?
EXPECTED: Critical Mach (Mcrit) is the lowest flight Mach number at which the airflow over any part of the aircraft first reaches the speed of sound. At Mcrit, a "shock wave" begins to form in a localized region — typically the upper surface of the wing at its thickest point. Above Mcrit, the shock wave grows and moves, causing buffet, increased drag (wave drag), and potentially Mach tuck. Critical Mach is approximately 0.72-0.82 for most swept-wing commercial transports. MMO (Mach Maximum Operating) is set below the point where serious aerodynamic effects begin.
KEY POINTS:
- The Mach number at which local airflow first reaches Mach 1.0
- Occurs on the upper wing surface at the point of maximum curvature
- Above Mcrit: shock waves form, wave drag increases, buffet begins
- Swept wings raise Mcrit compared to straight wings (the sweep reduces the effective component of airspeed perpendicular to the leading edge)
- Typical for commercial transports: Mcrit 0.72-0.82
AVOID: Confusing Mcrit with MMO. Missing that it applies to a local point on the aircraft, not the whole aircraft.

Q: How does aft CG affect aircraft performance and stability?
EXPECTED: Aft CG (within limits) improves performance by reducing the download required from the horizontal stabilizer to maintain level flight. This reduces trim drag and allows the aircraft to fly more efficiently. However, aft CG reduces longitudinal static stability — the aircraft is less stable and requires more pilot/autopilot input to maintain pitch. Recovery from unusual attitudes is more difficult. Forward CG (within limits) is opposite: slightly more stable but less efficient (more trim drag). Extreme aft CG beyond limits can lead to loss of control.
KEY POINTS:
- Aft CG (within limits): less stable but more efficient (less trim drag)
- Forward CG (within limits): more stable but less efficient (more trim drag)
- Aft CG: reduced stabilizer download needed, improves L/D
- Aft CG: less pitch stability, harder unusual attitude recovery
- Beyond aft limit: catastrophic loss of pitch control
AVOID: Saying aft CG is always better. Failing to note the stability penalty.

Q: What factors affect aircraft performance calculations?
EXPECTED: Performance (takeoff and landing distances, climb rates, ceiling) is affected by: temperature (higher temp = lower density = reduced performance), pressure altitude (higher = lower density), humidity (higher humidity = lower density — water vapor displaces denser air), aircraft weight (heavier = worse performance), runway slope (uphill takeoff = better stopping but longer takeoff roll), runway surface condition (wet, contaminated = longer stopping), wind (headwind = better takeoff/landing performance; tailwind = worse), anti-ice system on (bleed air extraction reduces engine thrust), flap setting (more flap = shorter field, lower climb gradient).
KEY POINTS:
- Temperature: higher = lower density = worse performance
- Pressure altitude: higher = less dense = worse performance
- Humidity: higher = slightly less dense (water vapor effect)
- Weight: heavier = longer distances, lower climb rate
- Wind: headwind helps; tailwind hurts
- Runway surface: wet/contaminated = longer stopping
- Runway slope: upslope = better stopping/worse takeoff; downslope = opposite
AVOID: Forgetting humidity (water vapor) effect. Missing anti-ice penalty.

Q: What happens to stall speed as weight increases?
EXPECTED: Stall speed increases as weight increases. This is because lift must equal weight for level flight (L = 1/2 ρV²SCL). To generate more lift (support greater weight), the aircraft must fly at a higher velocity, a higher angle of attack (up to CLmax), or both. For a fixed CLmax and air density, a heavier aircraft stalls at a higher indicated airspeed. VREF (approach speed) is calculated as 1.3 x Vstall in landing configuration, so it also increases with weight.
KEY POINTS:
- Stall speed increases with weight
- Lift equation: L = 1/2 ρV²SCL — heavier aircraft needs more V at same CLmax
- VREF = 1.3 x Vstall; therefore VREF also increases with weight
- Also applies to maneuvering: higher-weight aircraft has higher load-factor-adjusted stall speed
AVOID: Saying stall speed stays the same regardless of weight.

Q: What happens to TAS as you climb at constant IAS?
EXPECTED: True airspeed (TAS) increases as you climb at constant indicated airspeed (IAS). This is because air density decreases with altitude. IAS is derived from dynamic pressure (1/2 ρV²). As density decreases, the aircraft must travel faster (higher TAS) to produce the same dynamic pressure (same IAS). Rule of thumb: TAS increases approximately 2% per 1,000 feet of altitude. At FL350 (~7 km MSL), TAS might be ~460 kts for an IAS of ~280 kts.
KEY POINTS:
- Constant IAS + climbing = TAS increases
- Lower air density at altitude requires higher TAS to produce same dynamic pressure
- Rule of thumb: ~2% TAS increase per 1,000 ft
- At cruise altitude (~FL350), TAS can be 40-60% higher than IAS
AVOID: Saying TAS stays the same. Confusing IAS with TAS.

Q: What happens to Mach number as you descend at constant TAS?
EXPECTED: Mach number increases as you descend at constant TAS. Mach number is the ratio of TAS to the local speed of sound. The speed of sound in air depends on temperature (speed of sound = ~39 x √temperature in degrees Rankine, or approximately 661 kts at sea level). As you descend, temperature increases (below the tropopause), which increases the local speed of sound. A fixed TAS thus represents a lower fraction of the speed of sound — so Mach number decreases when descending at constant TAS. Wait — this is the opposite. Let me be precise: Mach = TAS / speed of sound. Temperature increases with descent → speed of sound increases → same TAS = LOWER Mach. So Mach decreases as you descend at constant TAS.
KEY POINTS:
- Mach = TAS / local speed of sound
- Speed of sound depends on temperature: warmer air = higher speed of sound
- Descending into warmer air: speed of sound increases → Mach decreases at constant TAS
- Opposite: climbing at constant TAS → entering colder air → speed of sound decreases → Mach increases
AVOID: Getting the relationship reversed.

Q: What is balanced field length?
EXPECTED: Balanced field length is the runway length where the accelerate-stop distance equals the accelerate-go distance at V1. It represents the optimum V1 that minimizes the required runway length. If V1 is set lower than the balanced field V1: the accelerate-stop distance is shorter (good) but the accelerate-go distance increases (more runway needed to get airborne). If V1 is higher: opposite. The balanced field condition is used in performance calculations to determine minimum runway requirements. Affected by: aircraft weight, temperature, pressure altitude, runway slope, wind.
KEY POINTS:
- Accelerate-stop distance = accelerate-go distance at balanced V1
- Represents the optimum V1 for minimum runway requirement
- Affected by: weight, temperature/pressure altitude, slope, wind, runway surface
- Used in performance charts to determine required field length
AVOID: Not knowing what "balanced" refers to (the two distances being equal).

Q: What is a stopway?
EXPECTED: A stopway is a rectangular area at the end of a runway, the same width as the runway, able to support the aircraft during an aborted takeoff without causing structural damage. It is designated and approved for decelerating an aircraft during an aborted takeoff. A stopway can be used to increase the accelerate-stop distance available (ASDA), but CANNOT be used as runway for the takeoff roll or for the takeoff distance available (TODA) for clearing obstacles. It is depicted on airport diagrams with a yellow "chevron" marking (the same markings used for blast pads, though blast pads are not designed to support aircraft weight for stopping).
KEY POINTS:
- At end of runway, same width, able to support aircraft weight for stopping
- Extends accelerate-stop distance (ASDA)
- Does NOT extend takeoff distance available (TODA) for climb
- NOT the same as a clearway (clearway extends TODA for obstacle clearance)
- Depicted as a paved or prepared surface beyond the runway end
AVOID: Confusing stopway with clearway. Thinking stopway credits accelerate-go distance.

Q: What is a clearway?
EXPECTED: A clearway is an area beyond the end of the runway, at least 500 feet wide, cleared of obstacles to a height below a specified slope. It can be used to extend the Takeoff Distance Available (TODA) for purposes of clearing obstacles during the initial climb (the airborne portion of the takeoff). A clearway does NOT help with the accelerate-stop distance — it is only useful if you are continuing the takeoff past the runway end (airborne). Maximum credit: clearway can be up to half the length of the takeoff run.
KEY POINTS:
- Area beyond runway end, at least 500 ft wide, clear of obstacles
- Extends Takeoff Distance Available (TODA) — airborne portion only
- Does NOT extend accelerate-stop distance (ASDA)
- Maximum clearway credit: up to 50% of the takeoff roll
- Different from stopway (which extends ASDA only)
AVOID: Confusing clearway with stopway. Thinking clearway helps with rejected takeoffs.

Q: What is Dutch roll and how do you recover from it?
EXPECTED: Dutch roll is an oscillatory motion consisting of a combined rolling and yawing motion that is out of phase with each other. It is caused when an aircraft has high sweep-back angle (good for speed) combined with high dihedral (good for stability) — the aircraft has strong roll stability but relatively weak directional stability. When disturbed from wings-level, the aircraft yaws left, then rolls right, then yaws right, then rolls left — in a corkscrew-like oscillation. Recovery: engage yaw damper (modern jets have yaw dampers that suppress Dutch roll automatically). Manual recovery: rudder inputs in the correct phase to damp the oscillation. Do NOT use ailerons — they tend to worsen Dutch roll.
KEY POINTS:
- Combined yaw-roll oscillation that is out of phase
- Caused by: high sweep + high dihedral (strong roll stability, weak directional stability)
- Yaw damper prevents and corrects Dutch roll on jet transports
- Manual recovery: use rudder (not ailerons) to damp the yaw
- Most dangerous if yaw damper fails at high altitude/speed
AVOID: Not knowing the recovery technique (rudder, not ailerons). Missing the yaw damper role.

Q: How does an aircraft fly / how is lift produced?
EXPECTED: Lift is generated by the interaction of the wing with the airflow. Two complementary mechanisms: (1) Bernoulli's principle: the wing is more curved on the top than the bottom; air flowing over the top travels a greater distance in the same time, therefore flows faster; faster flow = lower static pressure above the wing; pressure differential "pulls" the wing upward. (2) Newton's third law: the wing deflects the airflow downward (angle of attack); the reaction force is upward (lift). Both mechanisms are real and both contribute. Lift depends on: speed (V²), wing area (S), air density (ρ), and coefficient of lift (CL, which depends on angle of attack and wing shape): L = 1/2 ρV²SCL.
KEY POINTS:
- Bernoulli: pressure differential due to different flow speeds over/under wing
- Newton: wing deflects air downward, reaction is upward lift
- Lift equation: L = 1/2 ρV²SCL
- Factors: speed, air density, wing area, angle of attack
AVOID: Using only Bernoulli and ignoring Newton (or vice versa). Not knowing the lift equation.

Q: How does a jet / turbofan engine work?
EXPECTED: A turbofan engine works by: (1) Fan: a large front fan ingests air. Most of this air bypasses the core engine as "bypass flow" (provides much of the thrust in high-bypass turbofans). (2) Compressor: remaining air enters multi-stage axial or centrifugal compressors that increase pressure. (3) Combustion chamber (burner): compressed air is mixed with fuel and ignited; combustion dramatically increases temperature and volume. (4) Turbine: hot high-pressure gases expand through turbine stages, driving the compressor and fan. (5) Nozzle/exhaust: remaining hot gases exit at high velocity, providing the remainder of the thrust. High-bypass turbofans (B737, A320, etc.) have bypass ratios of 5:1 to 12:1 — most thrust from the fan, not the jet exhaust.
KEY POINTS:
- Fan: ingests and accelerates a large mass of air (bypass)
- Compressor: raises air pressure
- Combustor: adds fuel, ignites, rapid expansion
- Turbine: extracts energy to drive compressor and fan
- Nozzle/exhaust: remaining thrust from hot exhaust
- High-bypass: most thrust from fan bypass; more efficient, quieter
AVOID: Describing only the core (turbojet) without the fan (turbofan). Missing that high-bypass fan provides most of the thrust.

Q: How do you calculate wing sweep requirements (swept-wing design rationale)?
EXPECTED: Wing sweep is used to delay the formation of shock waves and raise the critical Mach number. A swept wing experiences a reduced component of airspeed perpendicular to the leading edge (the component that drives aerodynamic effects). The effective Mach number experienced by the wing cross-section is approximately: M_effective = M_flight x cos(sweep angle). So a 35-degree swept wing reduces the effective Mach by cos(35°) ≈ 0.82, raising the effective Mcrit. The penalty: swept wings have lower lift efficiency at low speeds (wing tip stall tendency, reduced CLmax), offset by slats and flaps. More sweep = higher cruise Mach capability; less sweep = better low-speed performance.
KEY POINTS:
- Sweep raises critical Mach by reducing effective Mach perpendicular to leading edge
- M_effective = M_flight x cos(sweep angle)
- ~30-35 degrees sweep typical for transonic commercial aircraft
- Penalty: worse low-speed CLmax; offset by leading-edge devices (slats, Krueger flaps)
- More sweep = faster cruise but worse field performance
AVOID: Not being able to explain the mechanism. Missing the low-speed penalty of high sweep.

Q: Why do planes fly so high?
EXPECTED: Two primary reasons: (1) Engine efficiency: turbofan engines operate most efficiently at high power settings close to maximum thrust. At sea level, maximum thrust greatly exceeds what is needed for level flight, so engines must be throttled back to low settings where they're inefficient. At altitude, reduced air density requires near-maximum power for level flight — engines run near their efficient operating point. (2) Reduced drag: thinner air at altitude = less aerodynamic drag = less thrust needed = less fuel burned per mile. Bonus: above ~30,000 ft, flights are above most weather systems, providing smoother ride and route predictability. Tropopause: approximately 36,000 ft; cruise altitudes are in lower stratosphere for many operations.
KEY POINTS:
- Engine efficiency: at cruise altitude, engines run near their optimal power setting
- Drag reduction: thinner air = less drag = less fuel per mile
- Smoother flight above most weather (above tropopause)
- Typical cruise: 30,000-42,000 ft
- Optimal altitude increases as weight decreases (step climbs as fuel burns)
AVOID: Saying "it's cooler up there" as a reason. Missing the engine efficiency explanation.

Q: What is a microburst and how do you escape one?
EXPECTED: A microburst is an intense localized convective downdraft from a thunderstorm cell that spreads outward on impact with the ground. It creates a severe windshear pattern: an aircraft on approach will first encounter an increasing headwind (aircraft climbs above glidepath), then the outflow boundary (rapidly shifting to a tailwind — aircraft sinks dramatically below glidepath), then the downdraft core itself (aircraft pushed down and forward). This sequence can cause a catastrophic loss of airspeed and altitude near the ground — the "microburst trap." Escape maneuver: TOGA power, rotate to windshear escape pitch attitude (aircraft-specific, typically 15-20 degrees), do NOT chase the glidepath, do NOT reduce power until clear.
KEY POINTS:
- Localized convective downdraft that spreads on the ground
- Severe windshear sequence: headwind → tailwind → downdraft
- On approach: aircraft climbs then sinks dramatically — below glidepath, underpowered
- ESCAPE: TOGA thrust immediately, rotate to windshear escape pitch
- Do NOT chase glidepath — fly attitude and let the energy build
- Do NOT reduce power until clearly above MSA and clear of shear
AVOID: Confusing microburst with macroburst (scale). Missing that you do NOT chase the glidepath in the escape.

Q: Walk me through the electrical system of a jet transport.
EXPECTED: Primary AC power comes from engine-driven Integrated Drive Generators (IDGs), one per engine. IDGs maintain constant frequency (400 Hz) regardless of engine speed via a constant speed drive unit. AC power (typically 115V, 3-phase, 400 Hz) powers most aircraft systems via main AC buses. Transformer-Rectifier Units (TRUs) convert AC to 28V DC for DC buses. Ground Power Unit (GPU) available on the ground. APU generator can power the aircraft on the ground or in flight (below certain altitude). Battery provides emergency DC power (30 minutes for essential systems). On some aircraft, a Ram Air Turbine (RAT) deploys if all generators fail to provide emergency hydraulic/electrical power.
KEY POINTS:
- Primary: Engine-driven IDGs → AC buses (115V/400Hz)
- TRUs convert AC to DC (28V)
- APU generator: backup on ground and at altitude
- External power (GPU): used on ground for pre-flight
- Battery: 30-min emergency backup for essential systems
- RAT: deploys for hydraulic/electrical emergency power if all generators fail
AVOID: Missing the IDG (the constant-speed drive mechanism). Not knowing the difference between AC and DC applications.

Q: Describe how a constant speed propeller works.
EXPECTED: A constant speed propeller uses a governor to maintain a pilot-selected RPM by automatically adjusting blade pitch. When the engine load increases (e.g., in a climb), the RPM tends to decrease. The governor senses the decrease and reduces oil pressure to the propeller hub, allowing counterweights and spring pressure to move the blades to a coarser pitch (less angle of attack), reducing aerodynamic drag on the blades and allowing RPM to recover. Conversely, if RPM increases (e.g., in a descent), the governor increases oil pressure to move blades to finer pitch. Failure mode: most systems default to fine pitch (high RPM, high drag) on oil pressure loss — the aircraft remains controllable but performance degrades.
KEY POINTS:
- Governor senses RPM and adjusts blade pitch to maintain selected RPM
- Oil pressure increases pitch (fine); spring/counterweight coarsens pitch
- High RPM tendency → governor reduces oil pressure → coarser pitch
- Low RPM tendency → governor increases oil pressure → finer pitch
- Failure mode: typically goes to fine pitch (feathering requires deliberate action)
AVOID: Describing a fixed-pitch propeller instead. Getting the fine/coarse pitch direction wrong.

Q: What is bleed air and what is it used for?
EXPECTED: Bleed air is high-pressure, high-temperature air extracted from the engine compressor stages (typically the 5th and/or 9th stage). It is "bled" from the engine flow and ducted to various aircraft systems. Primary uses: (1) Environmental Control System (ECS) — pressurization and air conditioning; (2) Wing and engine anti-ice systems (hot air through piccolo tubes in leading edges); (3) Hydraulic reservoir pressurization; (4) Water system pressurization; (5) Engine starting (starter uses high-pressure bleed from another engine or APU). Note: The Boeing 787 Dreamliner was a major departure from traditional bleed-air systems — it uses an "all-electric" architecture (electric anti-ice, electric ECS compressors) to improve efficiency.
KEY POINTS:
- High-pressure hot air extracted from engine compressor
- ECS: pressurization and air conditioning
- Anti-ice: wing leading edges and engine inlets
- Hydraulic reservoir pressurization
- Engine starting
- B787 exception: no bleed air — all-electric architecture
AVOID: Forgetting the B787 bleed-free exception if interviewing at an airline that operates 787s.

Q: What are the hydraulic systems on a large transport?
EXPECTED: Most large transports have two or three independent hydraulic systems (named by number or letter, e.g., Hydraulic System A and B on the B737). Each system has its own reservoir, pump(s), and serves different flight controls to ensure redundancy. For example, on the B737-800: System A powers left thrust reverser, ground spoilers, autopilot, nose wheel steering, normal brakes. System B powers right thrust reverser, flight spoilers, autopilot, alternate brakes. Overlap ensures no single failure loses all hydraulic function. Many systems have electric motor-driven backup pumps (ACMP) and engine-driven pumps (EDP).
KEY POINTS:
- Typically 2-3 independent hydraulic systems
- Each system powers different flight controls for redundancy
- Engine-driven pumps (EDP) are primary; electric motor pumps (ACMP) are backup
- Separate reservoirs prevent cross-contamination
- Critical systems (flight controls, brakes, steering) are cross-powered between systems
AVOID: Not knowing how many hydraulic systems your current aircraft has. Missing the redundancy purpose.

Q: Describe fuel planning and fuel system considerations for a long-haul flight.
EXPECTED: Minimum fuel load for Part 121: trip fuel + alternate fuel + final reserve (30 min at holding speed at 1,500 ft) + contingency (5% of trip fuel or 5 minutes, whichever greater) + taxi fuel + any additional fuel required (ETOPS, overwater, performance-limited alternate). Fuel system management: cross-feed valves allow any engine to draw from any tank; CG must be maintained within limits via fuel transfer; fuel temperature must be monitored above fuel freeze point (JET-A freezes at approximately -47°C); fuel quantity measured by capacitance probes.
KEY POINTS:
- Fuel breakdown: trip fuel + alternate + final reserve + contingency + taxi
- Final reserve: 30 min at holding speed at 1,500 ft AGL
- Contingency: 5% of trip fuel (higher of various calculations)
- ETOPS: additional fuel for single-engine divert from critical point
- Fuel temperature: must stay above freeze point (JET-A: ~-47°C)
- Cross-feed: any tank to any engine; used for balance and emergency
AVOID: Not knowing the components of required fuel. Forgetting the CG/fuel management aspect.

Q: Describe your aircraft's pressurization system.
EXPECTED: Commercial aircraft cabins are pressurized by bleed air (except B787) directed through the ECS (Environmental Control System). Outflow valves on the fuselage regulate cabin pressure by controlling how much air escapes. The system maintains a "cabin altitude" of approximately 6,000-8,000 feet MSL regardless of actual flight altitude. At 8,000 ft, differential pressure is approximately 8-9 PSI (varies by aircraft). The automatic pressurization controller uses inputs from: barometric altitude, flight mode, and programmed landing airport elevation to schedule the pressurization/depressurization profile. Manual backup is available.
KEY POINTS:
- Bleed air input through packs (ECS); outflow valves control the escape rate
- Cabin altitude maintained at ~6,000-8,000 ft MSL
- Differential pressure: ~8-9 PSI maximum
- Pressurization profile: automatic based on altitude and destination elevation
- Manual override available
- Rapid decompression: outflow valve failure, structural breach, or seal failure
AVOID: Not knowing the approximate cabin altitude maintained. Missing that outflow valves regulate the differential.

Q: What are V1, VR, V2, and VREF?
EXPECTED: V1: The takeoff decision speed. At V1, the first action to abort (reducing thrust) must have been initiated to stop on the remaining runway. After V1, the decision is made to fly. V1 ≥ VMCG (minimum control speed on ground) and ≤ VR. VR: Rotation speed. The speed at which the pilot begins to rotate to the takeoff attitude. V2: Takeoff safety speed. The minimum speed that must be achieved by 35 feet AGL (dry runway). Provides a defined climb performance with one engine failed. V2 ≥ 1.1 x Vmc (min control speed in air) and ≥ 1.13 x Vstall. VREF: Final approach reference speed. Typically 1.3 x Vstall in landing configuration. Used for final approach speed targeting.
KEY POINTS:
- V1: decision speed — after V1, commit to fly
- VR: rotation speed — begin rotation here
- V2: safety speed — must achieve by 35 ft AGL; provides OEI climb performance
- VREF: approach reference speed = 1.3 x Vstall in landing config
- V1 ≥ VMCG; V1 ≤ VR
AVOID: Not knowing the relationship between V1, VR, V2. Getting VREF calculation wrong.

Q: What is VA (maneuvering speed)?
EXPECTED: VA (maneuvering speed) is the speed below which the application of full control input will not exceed the aircraft's structural load limits because the wing will stall before the structural limit is reached. Above VA, full control deflection could overstress the airframe. Important nuance: this assumes ONE control input at a time — VA does NOT protect against simultaneous full control inputs in multiple axes. VA decreases with decreasing weight (lighter aircraft stall at lower speed, so the protection activates earlier). VA is NOT a fixed speed — it must be recalculated for the actual weight.
KEY POINTS:
- Speed below which full single control input won't structurally damage aircraft
- Wing stalls before limit load is reached at or below VA
- VA DECREASES with lighter weight
- Does NOT protect against simultaneous multiple full control inputs
- Turbulence penetration speed (often VB or VA): typically lower than MMO/VMO cruise
AVOID: Saying VA is a fixed number regardless of weight. Saying VA protects against all simultaneous full control inputs.

Q: What is VMO/MMO?
EXPECTED: VMO is Maximum Operating Speed (in knots IAS). MMO is Maximum Operating Mach Number. These are the maximum speeds at which the aircraft is certificated to be flown in normal operations. Exceeding VMO/MMO can result in: structural overstress, control reversal, Mach tuck, or high-speed buffet. The aircraft has warnings (overspeed aural/visual alert) below VMO/MMO. Recovery from exceedance: reduce thrust, deploy speedbrakes (if within limits), ease nose up. VMO/MMO transitions: at lower altitudes the IAS limit governs; at higher altitudes the Mach limit governs — they intersect at a specific altitude (the "crossover altitude").
KEY POINTS:
- VMO: maximum IAS (knots), applies at lower altitudes
- MMO: maximum Mach, applies at higher altitudes
- Crossover altitude: where IAS limit and Mach limit intersect
- Exceeding either: structural overstress, Mach tuck, high-speed buffet risk
- Overspeed warning activates before reaching VMO/MMO
AVOID: Thinking only one applies at all times. Missing the crossover altitude concept.

Q: What is VFE, VLE, and VLO?
EXPECTED: VFE: Maximum Flap Extended Speed. Each flap setting has its own VFE — exceeding it could damage the flaps due to aerodynamic loads. Speeds decrease as flap deployment increases (e.g., Flaps 1: 250 kts; Flaps 40: 165 kts). VLO: Maximum Landing Gear Operating Speed. The maximum speed at which the landing gear may be operated (extended OR retracted). Operating the gear above VLO risks gear door damage and structural failure. VLE: Maximum Landing Gear Extended Speed. The maximum speed at which the aircraft may be flown with the landing gear down and locked. VLE > VLO typically — once gear is down and locked, it can withstand higher speeds than during operation.
KEY POINTS:
- VFE: max speed with flaps at each setting — varies by flap position
- VLO: max speed to operate (extend/retract) the landing gear
- VLE: max speed with gear extended and locked (typically higher than VLO)
- Exceeding any: structural damage to gear, doors, or flaps
AVOID: Confusing VLO and VLE. Not knowing that VFE varies with flap setting.

Q: What is the constant speed propeller governor's failure mode?
EXPECTED: In most constant-speed propeller systems (turboprop and piston), a loss of oil pressure (which is used to move the blades toward fine pitch) causes the propeller to go to COARSE pitch (feathered direction) due to spring and counterweight action. This is a "fail-safe" design for in-flight engine failure — if an engine fails, the propeller will automatically tend toward feather (high pitch / fine blade angle, lowest drag), reducing the aerodynamic drag of the windmilling prop. Some older designs go to fine pitch on oil pressure loss (high RPM, high drag) — but modern turboprop systems are designed to go coarse (toward feather) on oil pressure failure.
KEY POINTS:
- Standard fail-to-coarse design: oil pressure loss → blades move toward feather (coarse pitch, low drag)
- This is the preferred failure mode for an engine-out situation
- Feathered prop significantly reduces drag compared to windmilling prop
- Some older piston systems may fail to fine pitch — know your specific aircraft
AVOID: Not knowing the failure mode direction. Not distinguishing turboprop from piston systems.

Q: Describe your aircraft's FMS / avionics.
EXPECTED: Tailor to your actual type rating. Generally: The Flight Management System (FMS/FMC) integrates navigation (GPS, VOR/DME, IRS), performance, and routing data. The FMS computes optimal routes, altitude profiles, and step climbs. Navigation modes: LNAV (lateral navigation) uses the FMS route; VNAV (vertical navigation) manages altitudes and descent profiles automatically. The FMS is loaded with the company route via ACARS or manual entry. CDU (Control Display Unit) is the pilot interface. Common avionics: TCAS II (traffic collision avoidance), GPWS/EGPWS (ground proximity warning), weather radar, ADS-B OUT (required by 2020 mandate).
KEY POINTS:
- FMS integrates navigation sensors (GPS, IRS, DME/DME) for position
- LNAV: follows programmed lateral route
- VNAV: manages programmed altitude profile and descent
- CDU: pilot interface for entering and monitoring FMS data
- Standard avionics: TCAS II, EGPWS, weather radar, ADS-B
AVOID: Generic answer unrelated to your actual aircraft type. Not knowing what LNAV and VNAV mean.

Q: What does each hydraulic system power on your aircraft?

Q: What is an APU and when is it used?
EXPECTED: The APU (Auxiliary Power Unit) is a small turbine engine located in the tail of most commercial aircraft. It runs on jet fuel from the aircraft's fuel tanks. The APU provides: (1) Electrical power on the ground (replaces GPU); (2) Bleed air for engine starting and air conditioning on the ground; (3) In-flight electrical backup (below certain altitude, typically FL250-FL400 depending on type); (4) In-flight air conditioning if a pack fails. The APU is shut down after engine start and typically not run in flight during normal operations (fuel cost, wear). Some aircraft (B787) have no APU bleed air function — APU only provides electrical power.
KEY POINTS:
- Small tail-mounted turbine engine
- Ground use: electrical power, engine starting bleed, air conditioning
- Flight use: backup electrical power, backup air conditioning
- Altitude limit: typically FL250-FL400 (type-specific) for starting
- B787: APU provides electricity only (no bleed air)
AVOID: Not knowing where the APU is located. Missing the in-flight emergency backup role.

Q: What happens if all engines fail — will the aircraft fall out of the sky?
EXPECTED: No — the aircraft will not simply fall out of the sky. Commercial jets have a lift-to-drag ratio of approximately 10:1 to 17:1, meaning they can glide 10-17 miles forward for every mile of altitude lost. At 36,000 feet (approximately 6 nautical miles), a commercial jet could glide roughly 60-100 NM. This gives 20-30 minutes of controlled flight time to find a suitable landing site. Additionally, most hydraulic and electrical systems can be powered by the RAT (Ram Air Turbine) which deploys automatically. Historical examples: US Airways 1549 (birds, Hudson River), Air Canada 143 (fuel miscalculation, Gimli Glider), Air Transat 236 (fuel leak, Azores).
KEY POINTS:
- Aircraft will GLIDE — will NOT fall straight down
- Typical L/D ratio: ~10:1 to 17:1 (10-17 NM per 6,000 ft)
- At FL360: approximately 60-100 NM glide range
- 20-30 minutes of flight time to find landing site
- RAT (Ram Air Turbine) deploys for emergency hydraulic/electrical power
- Historical examples: Air Canada 143 (Gimli Glider), UA 232, US Airways 1549
AVOID: Not knowing the approximate glide ratio. Missing the RAT role.

Q: What type of flaps does your aircraft use and how do they affect performance?
EXPECTED: Large commercial aircraft use a combination of trailing-edge flaps and leading-edge devices. Trailing edge: Fowler flaps are most common on jets — they extend rearward AND downward, increasing both wing area and camber simultaneously. This increases CLmax significantly. Leading edge: slats and Krueger flaps reduce stall speed by extending the leading edge forward, maintaining attached flow at higher angles of attack. Effect of flaps: lower stall speed (allows slower approach and landing speeds), higher drag (shorter landing distance via more deceleration), increased lift at low speeds, lower L/D ratio (burns more fuel at the same speed).
KEY POINTS:
- Fowler flaps: extend aft and down, increasing wing area AND camber
- Slats (leading edge): extend forward, delay stall, allow higher angle of attack
- Combined effect: lower stall speed, higher CLmax, more drag
- Flaps allow slower approach/landing speeds (shorter field required)
- Flaps increase drag: useful for approach but penalize climb gradient
AVOID: Not knowing the type of flaps on your aircraft. Missing the leading edge device contribution.

Q: What are the immediate actions for a rapid decompression?
EXPECTED: Immediate memory items (no checklist required until complete): (1) Crew oxygen masks ON and 100% — must be done within seconds at altitude (time of useful consciousness at FL350 is ~15-30 seconds); (2) Communications — confirm each pilot is on oxygen; (3) Emergency descent — deploy speedbrakes, reduce thrust to idle, descend at maximum rate to 10,000 ft (or MEA if higher); (4) Squawk 7700; (5) ATC declaration — "MAYDAY, rapid decompression, emergency descent"; (6) Confirm passenger oxygen deployment (cabin crew confirmation); (7) Divert to nearest suitable airport. Then: checklist, cabin crew status, passenger assessment.
KEY POINTS:
- OXYGEN MASKS ON IMMEDIATELY — seconds matter at altitude
- Emergency descent: speedbrakes, idle thrust, maximum rate to 10,000 ft or MEA
- Squawk 7700
- ATC MAYDAY call
- Confirm passenger O2 deployment
- Divert to nearest suitable airport
AVOID: Any delay on oxygen masks — 15-30 seconds of useful consciousness at FL350 is not a drill. Not knowing the time of useful consciousness.

Q: What is a RAM Air Turbine (RAT) and when does it deploy?
EXPECTED: A Ram Air Turbine (RAT) is a small turbine (propeller-like device) that deploys from the fuselage in emergency conditions. Driven by the airstream, it generates electrical and/or hydraulic power to maintain essential flight controls and instruments when main power sources (engines, APU) are unavailable. On most large transports, the RAT provides enough power for: essential electrical buses, flight controls (via hydraulic or electric actuators), and essential instruments. Automatic deployment: when both main AC buses lose power, the RAT deploys automatically. It can also be manually deployed.
KEY POINTS:
- Small air-driven turbine that deploys from fuselage
- Driven by the aircraft's motion through the air (ram air)
- Provides emergency hydraulic and/or electrical power
- Automatic deployment: when main electrical system fails
- Provides enough power for essential flight controls and instruments
- Not full power — only essential buses; many non-essential systems inoperative
AVOID: Confusing RAT with APU. Not knowing what systems the RAT powers.

Q: What is BR on a METAR?
EXPECTED: BR is the METAR code for mist. Mist is defined as suspended water droplets or ice crystals reducing visibility to between 5/8 of a statute mile and 6 statute miles. When visibility is 5/8 SM or less, the code changes to FG (fog). Other common METAR precipitation codes to know: FG=fog, HZ=haze, FU=smoke, RA=rain, SN=snow, TS=thunderstorm, DZ=drizzle, GR=hail, FZRA=freezing rain, SH=shower.
KEY POINTS:
- BR = mist (5/8 SM to 6 SM visibility)
- FG = fog (visibility <5/8 SM or <=1/4 SM depending on ICAO/FAA definition)
- Other important codes: RA=rain, SN=snow, TS=thunderstorm, GR=hail, FZRA=freezing rain
- Present weather in METARs: intensity prefix (- light, no modifier=moderate, + heavy)
AVOID: Confusing BR (mist) with FG (fog). Not knowing other common present weather codes.

Q: What does TS mean on a METAR?
EXPECTED: TS = Thunderstorm. In a METAR, TS indicates a thunderstorm is occurring at or in the vicinity of the airport. It may be followed by other weather descriptors: TSRA = thunderstorm with rain; TSGR = thunderstorm with hail; TSRA+ = heavy thunderstorm with rain. The VC prefix means in vicinity (within 5-10 miles of the airport): VCTS = thunderstorm in vicinity. Thunderstorm information also may appear in the Remarks (RMK) section with location. Thunderstorms are the most dangerous weather phenomenon for aviation — always avoid by a minimum of 20 NM.
KEY POINTS:
- TS = thunderstorm (currently occurring at the airport)
- TSRA = thunderstorm with rain; TSGR = with hail
- VCTS = thunderstorm in the vicinity (5-10 miles)
- Thunderstorm in remarks may give more location detail
AVOID: Not recognizing TS in compound weather codes.

Q: Describe a cold front versus a warm front.
EXPECTED: Cold front: a boundary where a cold air mass is actively replacing a warm air mass. Cold fronts have steep slope (1:50 to 1:100), so they typically produce a narrow band of severe weather — intense but brief. Weather: cumulonimbus, heavy rain, strong winds, thunderstorms, rapid visibility changes. Passes quickly. Post-frontal: cold, clear, good visibility, northerly/northwesterly winds. Warm front: a boundary where a warm air mass is replacing a cold air mass. Warm fronts have a shallow slope (1:150 to 1:300), so they bring widespread, prolonged weather: stratus clouds, drizzle, freezing rain, fog, low ceilings over hundreds of miles. Passes slowly.
KEY POINTS:
- Cold front: steep slope, narrow severe weather band, fast-moving, cumulonimbus, strong winds
- Warm front: shallow slope, widespread low ceilings/drizzle/stratus, slow-moving, fog risk
- Cold front passage: rapid weather change, then clear and cold
- Warm front passage: gradual clearing, followed by warm humid air
- Freezing rain most common on warm fronts (rain falling through below-freezing surface air)
AVOID: Confusing which is more dangerous for aviation. Missing the freezing rain risk of warm fronts.

Q: What is clear air turbulence (CAT)?
EXPECTED: Clear Air Turbulence (CAT) is turbulence that occurs in cloudless airspace, typically associated with jet streams and wind shear at high altitudes. Most common near the tropopause and the edges of jet streams where wind shear is greatest. CAT cannot be detected by weather radar (no precipitation to return the signal) — it relies entirely on PIREPs, crew reports, turbulence forecasts (SIGMETs), and PIREP-based weather products. CAT can be severe without warning, causing passenger and crew injuries. Detection technology: LIDAR and wake turbulence sensors are being developed. The primary avoidance technique is using PIREPs and altitude changes to avoid shear zones.
KEY POINTS:
- Turbulence in cloudless air, no visual warning
- Most common near jet streams and tropopause
- Cannot be detected by weather radar
- Detection relies on PIREPs, SIGMETs, forecasting models
- Avoidance: altitude changes, lateral deviations based on PIREPs
- Always keep seatbelt sign on cruise as protection
AVOID: Saying it can be detected by radar (it cannot — no moisture).

Q: What is a SIGMET and when is it issued?
EXPECTED: SIGMET (Significant Meteorological Information) is an NWS advisory issued for conditions that are potentially hazardous to ALL aircraft. Non-convective SIGMETs cover: (1) Severe or extreme turbulence; (2) Severe or extreme icing NOT associated with thunderstorms; (3) Widespread duststorms or sandstorms lowering visibility below 3 miles; (4) Volcanic ash. Convective SIGMETs (WST) are issued by the Aviation Weather Center for: (1) Severe thunderstorms with surface winds ≥50 knots, hail ≥3/4 inch, tornadoes; (2) Embedded thunderstorms; (3) Lines of thunderstorms ≥60 miles long; (4) Areas of thunderstorms covering ≥40% of an area of 3,000+ square miles. Convective SIGMETs are issued for continental US only.
KEY POINTS:
- SIGMET: hazardous to ALL aircraft
- Conditions: severe turbulence, severe icing, dust/sandstorm, volcanic ash
- Convective SIGMET (WST): severe thunderstorms, embedded TS, lines, area coverage
- Valid period: 4 hours (6 for volcanic ash/tropical cyclones); Convective SIGMETs 2 hours
- Legal requirement: pilots must check for SIGMETs along their route
AVOID: Confusing SIGMET with AIRMET. Not knowing the difference between SIGMET and Convective SIGMET.

Q: What is an AIRMET and how does it differ from a SIGMET?
EXPECTED: AIRMET (Airman's Meteorological Information) is less severe than a SIGMET. AIRMETs are issued for conditions potentially hazardous to SMALLER aircraft (light singles and twins) or any aircraft not equipped for certain conditions. Three types: AIRMET Sierra (S): IFR conditions (ceiling <1,000 ft / vis <3 SM over 50% of area), extensive mountain obscuration. AIRMET Tango (T): moderate turbulence, sustained surface winds ≥30 knots, low-level wind shear. AIRMET Zulu (Z): moderate icing and freezing level heights. AIRMETs are valid for 6 hours. SIGMETs are for severe or extreme conditions hazardous to all aircraft.
KEY POINTS:
- AIRMET: hazardous primarily to smaller/lighter aircraft
- Sierra (S): IFR conditions, mountain obscuration
- Tango (T): moderate turbulence, surface winds, wind shear
- Zulu (Z): moderate icing, freezing levels
- Valid 6 hours; updated with SFO amendments as needed
- SIGMET = more severe, all aircraft; AIRMET = moderate, smaller aircraft
AVOID: Confusing the three AIRMET types. Not knowing S/T/Z classification.

Q: What is a convective SIGMET?
EXPECTED: A Convective SIGMET (WST) is issued by the Aviation Weather Center for convective weather that is hazardous to all aircraft. Conditions triggering issuance: (1) Severe thunderstorms with surface winds ≥50 kts, hail at surface ≥3/4 inch, or tornadoes; (2) Embedded thunderstorms; (3) Lines of thunderstorms ≥60 miles long; (4) Area of thunderstorms covering ≥40% of an area ≥3,000 sq. miles. Issued hourly (at :55 past the hour) and as special reports. Valid 2 hours. Covers contiguous US. Numbered from 1-99, then reset. Airspace in Convective SIGMET areas should be avoided.
KEY POINTS:
- Issued for severe convection (all aircraft hazard)
- Triggers: severe thunderstorms (winds 50+, hail 3/4"+), embedded TS, line TS 60+ mi, area TS 40%+
- Issued hourly at :55 past the hour + special reports
- Valid 2 hours
- Numbered 1-99 sequentially
AVOID: Not distinguishing from regular SIGMET. Forgetting it covers only the contiguous US.

Q: What is the correlation between temperature and lightning?
EXPECTED: Higher temperatures increase lightning frequency because warmer air has more energy to fuel convective updrafts. Stronger updrafts in cumulonimbus clouds create more ice crystal collisions that separate electrical charge — smaller positively-charged ice crystals rise to the top of the cloud while larger negatively-charged graupel falls to the bottom. The charge separation builds until the difference creates a lightning discharge. In warmer, more unstable air masses, updraft velocities are greater, charge separation is more rapid, and lightning is more frequent. This is why tropical thunderstorms produce more lightning than polar thunderstorms.
KEY POINTS:
- Higher temperature → more atmospheric instability → stronger updrafts
- Stronger updrafts → more ice crystal/graupel collisions → faster charge separation
- Higher charge separation rate → more frequent lightning
- Tropical thunderstorms: more lightning than polar due to higher surface temperatures
- Cold front thunderstorms often more severe but briefer than warm-season storms
AVOID: Not being able to explain the charge separation mechanism.

Q: Describe high and low pressure systems — formation, weather, wind flow.
EXPECTED: High pressure (anticyclone): air descends and diverges at the surface. Descending air warms adiabatically → clear, stable, good visibility. Surface winds flow clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere (anticyclonic). Weather: calm, clear, often fog in winter due to radiative cooling under a clear sky. Low pressure (cyclone): air converges at the surface and rises. Rising air cools adiabatically → condensation, clouds, precipitation, instability. Surface winds flow counterclockwise in the Northern Hemisphere (cyclonic). Weather: clouds, precipitation, turbulence. Lows track generally west to east across North America.
KEY POINTS:
- High: descending air, surface divergence, clear, stable, clockwise winds (NH)
- Low: rising air, surface convergence, clouds/precipitation, counterclockwise winds (NH)
- Buys Ballot's Law: stand with the wind at your back in NH — low pressure is to your left
- Lows track eastward; can deepen rapidly (bombogenesis)
AVOID: Getting wind flow direction wrong (common mistake). Forgetting the relationship between pressure and weather.

Q: What is a VOR and how does it work?
EXPECTED: VOR (VHF Omnidirectional Range) is a ground-based navigation radio beacon operating on frequencies between 108.0 and 117.95 MHz. The station transmits two signals simultaneously: a reference signal (phase) and a variable signal (rotating in azimuth at 30 rpm). The aircraft's VOR receiver compares the phase difference between these two signals to determine the magnetic bearing FROM the station (the radial). Radials are measured FROM the station: if you're on the 090 radial, you are due east of the station. VOR accuracy: ±2 degrees (Class T - terminal; less range). Types: VORTAC adds a DME (distance) component from the collocated TACAN; VOR/DME includes civil DME collocated.
KEY POINTS:
- VHF Omnidirectional Range: 108.0-117.95 MHz
- Two signals: reference (fixed) and variable (rotating) — phase difference = bearing
- Radials: bearings FROM the station (000-359)
- Accuracy: ±2 degrees of bearing
- VORTAC: VOR + DME (military TACAN collocated)
- VOR/DME: VOR + civil DME
AVOID: Saying radials are measured TO the station. Not knowing the frequency range.

Q: What is GPS RAIM?
EXPECTED: RAIM (Receiver Autonomous Integrity Monitoring) is a GPS receiver function that assesses the integrity (reliability) of the GPS signal. RAIM checks whether there are enough satellites in view and whether the satellite geometry is sufficient to provide reliable positioning. If RAIM is unavailable (too few satellites, poor geometry, or a satellite is faulty), the receiver alerts the pilot and the GPS may not be used for navigation or approaches. For IFR operations, RAIM availability must be checked before departure — especially for approaches. RAIM prediction tools (ForeFlight, Garmin, FAA website) can predict outages. Fault Detection and Exclusion (FDE) is an advanced RAIM variant that can identify and exclude a faulty satellite.
KEY POINTS:
- RAIM monitors GPS signal integrity (enough satellites, good geometry)
- Alert if RAIM is unavailable: GPS cannot be used for IFR navigation
- Must check RAIM availability before IFR departure, especially for GPS approaches
- FDE (Fault Detection and Exclusion): advanced RAIM that can exclude a faulty satellite
- RAIM prediction tools available for pre-flight planning
AVOID: Not knowing what RAIM stands for or what it monitors.

Q: Describe an RNAV/RNP approach.
EXPECTED: An RNAV approach uses area navigation (GPS/FMS) rather than ground-based navaids to guide the aircraft to the runway. An RNP approach adds onboard performance monitoring — the aircraft continuously monitors its navigation accuracy and alerts if it exceeds the required performance (e.g., RNP 0.1 means accuracy within 0.1 NM 95% of the time with alerting at 2x that value). RNP AR (Authorization Required) approaches are the most precise — they allow curved (radius-to-fix) approaches to airports surrounded by terrain, using vertical guidance (LPV or similar) and are precision-like with decision altitudes as low as 200 ft. The authorization to fly RNP AR requires specific aircraft equipment, training, and OpSpec approval.
KEY POINTS:
- RNAV approach: GPS/FMS navigation, not tied to ground navaids
- RNP adds performance monitoring and alerting (onboard integrity check)
- RNP value: e.g., RNP 0.1 = within 0.1 NM 95% of time
- RNP AR: curved approaches, lowest minimums, authorization required
- LPV (Localizer Performance with Vertical guidance): GPS approach with DA, similar to ILS Cat I minimums
AVOID: Treating RNAV and RNP as the same. Missing the performance monitoring component of RNP.

Q: Explain holding entry types — parallel, teardrop, direct.
EXPECTED: The three standard holding entry procedures are based on the aircraft's heading relative to the holding fix: Direct entry: aircraft heading is within the sector that would keep it on the holding side; turn right (for standard right-hand holding) to the inbound course. Teardrop entry: aircraft heading is more than 180 but less than the direct entry sector boundary; fly to the fix, turn to a heading 30 degrees on the holding side of the outbound course, fly outbound for approximately 1 minute, then turn in the direction of the holding pattern to intercept the inbound course. Parallel entry: aircraft heading is on the non-holding side; fly outbound parallel to the inbound course for 1 minute, turn 180 degrees toward the holding side, then intercept the inbound course.
KEY POINTS:
- Three sectors divide 360 degrees relative to the inbound course
- Direct entry: fly directly to the fix and turn to inbound
- Teardrop entry: fly to fix, turn 30 degrees on holding side of outbound, then turn back
- Parallel entry: fly outbound parallel for 1 minute, 180-turn, intercept inbound
- Standard holding: right turns (left turns may be published)
- Timing: 1 minute outbound below FL140; 1.5 minutes at or above FL140
AVOID: Not being able to draw or describe all three entries. Getting the teardrop angle wrong (it's 30 degrees toward the holding side).

Q: How do you fly a DME arc?
EXPECTED: A DME arc is a curved flight path at a constant distance (in NM) from a VOR/DME station. To fly a DME arc: set the OBS to the current radial. Lead the arc turns by approximately 0.5 NM per 10 kts groundspeed for each turn. As you fly, periodically check the DME and OBS — if DME is increasing, turn toward the station; if decreasing, turn away. Use a series of 10-degree turns ("cutting across the chord") to approximate the arc. Or use lateral navigation (LNAV) if the arc is in the FMS. The arc is typically flown at a consistent bank angle (5-10 degrees). Know your lead-in radial for turning onto the final approach course.
KEY POINTS:
- Constant DME from VOR station
- Lead turns by ~0.5 NM per 10 kts of groundspeed
- Monitor DME and adjust: too far = turn in; too close = turn away
- 10-degree incremental turns to follow the arc
- LNAV can fly the arc automatically if programmed
- Know the lead radial for transitioning to the final approach course
AVOID: Not knowing how to manually fly the arc. Missing the lead-in to final approach.

Q: What is a displaced threshold and how is it depicted?
EXPECTED: A displaced threshold is a threshold that is not located at the physical beginning of the runway pavement. The area before the displaced threshold (between the runway beginning and the threshold) is available for takeoff roll and landing rollout/taxi, but NOT for landing touchdown. The displaced threshold is depicted on the airport diagram and on the runway itself by white arrows pointing to the threshold bar and large white numbers. Reasons for displacement: obstacles off the end of the approach path, pavement strength not sufficient for landing, noise abatement. Pilots must aim for the threshold bar on landing, not the beginning of the pavement.
KEY POINTS:
- Threshold not at the start of the runway pavement
- Area before displaced threshold: usable for takeoff and rollout, NOT for landing
- Depicted by white arrows on pavement leading to the threshold bar
- Reasons: obstacles, noise abatement, pavement condition
- Landing touchdown must be at or after the threshold
AVOID: Thinking displaced threshold area cannot be used at all. Confusing with declared distances.

Q: What is a blast pad / stopway and how is it depicted on an airport diagram?
EXPECTED: A blast pad (also called an overrun area) is a paved surface at the runway end designed to protect the area from jet blast erosion. It is NOT designed to support aircraft weight for stopping (unlike a stopway). It is NOT usable for takeoff, landing, or stopping — it's only there to prevent erosion. Depicted on airport diagrams with yellow chevron markings. A stopway IS designed to support the aircraft during an aborted takeoff and extends the accelerate-stop distance available. Blast pad/overrun areas look similar to stopways but are distinguished by their engineering purpose and whether they can support aircraft weight.
KEY POINTS:
- Blast pad/overrun: protects against jet blast erosion; NOT usable by aircraft for stopping
- Stopway: engineered to support aircraft weight during rejected takeoff
- Both depicted with chevron markings on airport diagrams
- Blast pad: yellow chevron markings; NOT credited in performance calculations
- Stopway: can be credited in accelerate-stop distance (ASDA)
AVOID: Confusing blast pad with stopway in performance calculations.

Q: What is a runway incursion hot spot?
EXPECTED: A runway incursion hot spot is a location on an airport where there is a historical risk of runway incursion or collision — often due to confusing taxiway geometry, poor sight lines, signage complexity, or traffic patterns that create conflict risks. Hot spots are identified by the FAA and published on airport diagrams as numbered circles (HS 1, HS 2, etc.) with brief descriptions of the specific hazard. Pilots must be extra vigilant at hot spots — clear taxi instructions, explicit read-back, hold short line compliance, and caution at night and in low visibility.
KEY POINTS:
- Locations on airport with historical runway incursion risk
- Published on airport diagrams as numbered circles (HS 1, HS 2, etc.)
- Require extra vigilance: complex geometry, poor visibility, confusing signs
- Pilots should brief hot spots during taxi
- ATC may highlight hot spots in taxi clearances
AVOID: Not knowing what a hot spot is or where they are depicted.

Q: How do you read altitude restrictions on a SID or STAR?
EXPECTED: On SID/STAR charts, altitude restrictions use symbolic notation: "AT OR ABOVE" is shown as a number with an underline (e.g., ___5000). "AT OR BELOW" is shown as a number with an overline/bar on top (written as 5000 with a line above). "AT" (exact altitude) is shown with both underline and overline. Speed restrictions are shown in parentheses (e.g., 250 KIAS below 10,000 ft). Mandatory altitude restrictions must be met. Expected altitude restrictions are issued by ATC and expected to be assigned. Recommended restrictions are not mandatory but should be used for planning.
KEY POINTS:
- At or above: number with underline (floor restriction)
- At or below: number with overline (ceiling restriction)
- At: number with both overline and underline (exact altitude)
- Speed restrictions: shown in parentheses
- Mandatory = must comply unless ATC amends
AVOID: Getting at or above and at or below symbols confused.

Q: What is the difference between mandatory, recommended, and expected altitude symbols on charts?
EXPECTED: On approach and departure charts: Mandatory altitude restriction: Bold text, underline/overline combination or specific published notation — must be complied with. Recommended altitude: Shown without a box, typically as a suggested altitude for obstacle clearance or ATC coordination — not required. Expected altitude: Published as an expectation of what ATC will assign — used for planning purposes and for lost communications procedures. Important for lost communications: fly the expected altitude if ATC-assigned altitude hasn't been received. Mandatory altitudes have legal standing; expected/recommended do not (unless assigned by ATC).
KEY POINTS:
- Mandatory: must comply (legally binding unless ATC amends)
- Recommended: advisory — good planning but not required
- Expected: what ATC plans to assign — used in lost comms procedures
- Lost communications: fly expected altitude until last assigned
AVOID: Treating all three types as mandatory.

Q: How do you read a METAR and use it for flight planning?
EXPECTED: METAR = Meteorological Aerodrome Report. Standard format: station ID, date/time (UTC), auto/manual flag, wind (direction/speed in knots; variable and gust if applicable), visibility, runway visual range, present weather, sky condition (cloud layers with coverage and height), temperature/dewpoint (Celsius), altimeter setting, remarks. Example: KORD 221356Z 27015G25KT 10SM FEW030 SCT080 BKN120 12/04 A2998 RMK AO2 = Chicago O'Hare, 22nd at 1356Z, winds 270 at 15 gusting 25, 10 SM visibility, clouds at 3,000 ft few, 8,000 ft scattered, 12,000 ft broken, temp 12°C dewpoint 4°C, altimeter 29.98. Used to: verify destination weather meets minimums at ETA, assess alternate options, identify icing/turbulence potential, check altimeter setting.
KEY POINTS:
- Format: station, time, wind, visibility, present weather, clouds, temp/dew, altimeter, remarks
- Wind: direction from (true or magnetic per ICAO/FAA) / speed in knots / gust
- Visibility: statute miles (US); meters (ICAO)
- Sky condition: FEW (1-2 oktas), SCT (3-4 oktas), BKN (5-7 oktas), OVC (8 oktas)
- Temperature/dewpoint: Celsius; small spread = fog risk
AVOID: Not knowing cloud coverage definitions. Confusing Celsius and Fahrenheit in planning.

Q: What is the tropopause and why is it significant for flight?
EXPECTED: The tropopause is the boundary between the troposphere (below) and the stratosphere (above). In the troposphere, temperature decreases with altitude. At the tropopause, temperature stops decreasing and either stabilizes or begins increasing (stratosphere has a temperature inversion due to ozone absorption). At mid-latitudes, the tropopause is typically at 35,000-45,000 feet; at the poles, lower (~25,000 ft); at the equator, higher (~55,000 ft). Significance for aviation: (1) Temperature stabilizes, which affects speed of sound and Mach number; (2) Jet streams form in the vicinity of the tropopause; (3) Most weather systems are contained in the troposphere; (4) CAT is most prevalent near the tropopause; (5) Cruise altitudes are often just below the tropopause.
KEY POINTS:
- Boundary between troposphere and stratosphere
- Temperature stops decreasing at the tropopause
- Mid-latitude location: ~35,000-45,000 ft; lower near poles, higher at equator
- Jet streams form in this region
- Most weather confined to troposphere
- CAT most prevalent near tropopause
AVOID: Not knowing the altitude range. Missing the jet stream / CAT connection.

Q: What is mountain wave turbulence?
EXPECTED: Mountain wave turbulence (lee wave turbulence) forms when strong winds (usually >25-30 kts) blow roughly perpendicular to a mountain ridge. The flow is disrupted and forms a standing wave pattern on the downwind (lee) side of the mountains. The wave can extend far downwind and to high altitudes. Features: smooth wave region in the wave crests; turbulent zones in the wave troughs and beneath the wave, especially in areas of rotor turbulence (a breaking wave that spins like a roller, extremely violent). Lenticular clouds (lens-shaped, "standing" altocumulus) indicate the wave crests. Rotor clouds below the lenticulars indicate the most violent turbulence zone. Avoidance: fly high above the wave tops, or avoid the lee side entirely.
KEY POINTS:
- Forms on the lee side of mountains with strong perpendicular winds
- Rotor turbulence: most violent, found below the wave level
- Lenticular (lens-shaped) clouds mark wave crests — don't fly through them
- Wave can extend hundreds of miles downwind and to very high altitudes
- Avoidance: fly above the wave or avoid the lee side
AVOID: Not knowing the rotor turbulence concept. Missing the lenticular cloud indicator.

Q: What is wind shear and how is it detected?
EXPECTED: Wind shear is a change in wind velocity (speed and/or direction) over a short distance. It can occur vertically (different wind at different altitudes) or horizontally (different wind at different locations at the same altitude). Low-level wind shear (LLWS) is most hazardous because it occurs in the critical phases of flight (approach and departure below 2,000 ft AGL). Detection: (1) PIREPs from preceding aircraft; (2) ATIS/AWOS wind reports; (3) Low Level Wind Shear Alert System (LLWAS) at equipped airports; (4) Onboard weather radar (shows precipitation movement, not dry shear); (5) Airborne wind shear detection systems (forward-looking or reactive). Reactive system: triggers when shear is encountered; forward-looking: predictive LIDAR.
KEY POINTS:
- Change in wind velocity over a short distance (speed/direction)
- Most dangerous when encountered in terminal area during approach/departure
- Detection: PIREPs, LLWAS, ATIS, onboard predictive systems
- Microburst is a specific type of intense low-altitude wind shear
- If wind shear encountered: execute escape maneuver (TOGA, maintain attitude)
AVOID: Confusing wind shear with turbulence (shear can occur in smooth air). Missing LLWAS as a detection method.

Q: What is a microburst and how does it differ from a macroburst?
EXPECTED: A microburst is a localized intense downdraft that affects an area less than 4 km (2.5 miles) in horizontal extent, with winds exceeding 45 kts differential, lasting 2-5 minutes. Extremely hazardous at low altitudes due to rapid windshear sequence. A macroburst covers a larger area (4-10 km or more) with comparable downdraft intensity but less concentration. The primary danger in both is the rapid wind shift from headwind to tailwind — the "microburst trap." Wet microbursts are associated with heavy precipitation and are detectable on weather radar. Dry microbursts occur with rain that evaporates before reaching the ground — virga — and can occur in clear conditions, making them harder to detect.
KEY POINTS:
- Microburst: <4 km horizontal extent, >45 kts wind differential, 2-5 minutes duration
- Macroburst: >4 km extent, longer duration, similar vertical wind structure
- Dry microburst: rain evaporates (virga) — no visible precipitation; harder to detect
- Wet microburst: associated with heavy precipitation, detectable by radar
- Both create dangerous windshear sequence for aircraft on approach/departure
AVOID: Not knowing the wet vs. dry distinction. Getting the size definitions wrong.

Q: What is icing certification (known ice vs flight into known icing)?
EXPECTED: An aircraft certified for flight in "known icing conditions" has been tested and certified to safely operate in moderate icing conditions with its ice protection systems operating. This is NOT the same as "all-weather" or unlimited icing capability. "Known ice" means the aircraft and its systems have been tested to FAR Part 25 icing appendix standards. VFR flight into known icing is prohibited for most aircraft. A pilot cannot legally fly into conditions where ice formation is likely unless the aircraft is certified for such operations. FAR 91.9 prohibits operating an aircraft contrary to its limitations. Airlines operating under Part 121 have specific operations specs authorizing operations in icing conditions.
KEY POINTS:
- "Known ice" certified: tested per FAR 25 Appendix C icing envelope
- Certification allows flight into defined icing conditions (not unlimited)
- Part 121 airlines: Ops Specs authorize specific icing operations
- Anti-ice vs. de-ice: anti-ice prevents formation; de-ice removes accumulated ice
- Pilot must activate ice protection before ice accumulates (anti-ice)
- FIKI (Flight Into Known Icing) certification required on the specific aircraft
AVOID: Thinking "known ice certified" means the aircraft can handle any icing. Confusing anti-ice with de-ice.

Q: What is a NOTAM and how do you check them?
EXPECTED: A NOTAM (Notice to Air Missions, formerly Notice to Airmen) is a notice containing information concerning the establishment, condition, or change to any aeronautical facility, service, procedure, or hazard. NOTAMs cover: runway/taxiway closures, navaids out of service, airspace restrictions (TFRs), lighting outages, bird hazards, construction, instrument approach procedures with changes, and much more. Pilots must check NOTAMs for departure, destination, and alternate airports, as well as any Special Use Airspace (SUAs) or TFRs along the route. Checked via: ForeFlight, ATIS broadcast (limited), FAA NOTAM Search (notams.faa.gov), dispatch provided NOTAM summary for airline ops.
KEY POINTS:
- Official notice of changes to aeronautical facilities, procedures, and hazards
- Types: D (distant), L (local), FDC (Flight Data Center — regulatory), TFR
- Must check for departure, destination, alternates, and en-route airspace
- Part 121: dispatch provides NOTAM summary; crew must review it
- ATIS/D-ATIS may include significant NOTAMs
- Check via EFB (ForeFlight, Jeppesen), dispatch paperwork, or FAA website
AVOID: Not checking TFRs. Forgetting FDC NOTAMs (which include amended approaches and airspace changes).

Q: How do you prepare for a flight?
EXPECTED: Systematic multi-phase answer. Night before (if possible): review route, check preliminary weather, review destination and alternate NOTAMs, review any aircraft MEL items from dispatch. Day of: ATIS, updated weather (METARs, TAFs, PIREPs, SIGMETs), aircraft pre-flight inspection, review dispatch release and W&B, review NOTAMs for departure/destination/alternate, review SIDs/STARs/approaches, brief the crew (CRM, threats, special considerations). This should sound like a professional, not a casual list.
KEY POINTS:
- Weather: ATIS, METARs, TAFs, PIREPs, SIGMETs, prognostic charts
- NOTAMs: departure, destination, alternate, and TFRs
- Dispatch release review: W&B, fuel, MEL items, route
- Aircraft pre-flight inspection
- Approach brief: approach plate review, minimums, missed approach
- Crew brief: roles, threats, communication norms, special considerations
AVOID: "I check the weather and do the walk-around." Too superficial. Missing the crew briefing.

Q: How do you manage fatigue?
EXPECTED: Multi-phase fatigue management: pre-trip (consistent sleep schedule, limit alcohol before trips, good nutrition), in-trip (maximize rest at layovers, use controlled rest procedures when legal, maintain regular meal schedule), tactical (if fatigued: report it — fatigue is protected under most airlines and Part 117 intent). Note the key principle: legal does not equal fit. FAR Part 117 provides the legal framework but the individual pilot is the final judge of fitness. Never fly when genuinely impaired by fatigue.
KEY POINTS:
- Pre-trip: sleep schedule, alcohol avoidance, nutrition
- Layover: maximize sleep time; treat rest as a professional responsibility
- In-flight: sterile cockpit in critical phases; stay engaged; monitor each other
- If fatigued: call it — it's protected; document it
- Never fly legally tired if you're genuinely impaired
- Know Part 117 WOCL concept and its effect on FDP limits
AVOID: "I drink coffee." Not addressing the fatigue call option. Not knowing Part 117 framework.

Q: How do you handle stress?
EXPECTED: Show you have active, evidence-based stress management techniques — not just "I stay calm." Techniques: pre-flight mental preparation (review the flight, anticipate threats, brief contingencies), controlled breathing (tactical breathing: inhale 4 counts, hold 4, exhale 4 — used by military and airline pilots), physical fitness and sleep as stress buffers, debriefing after stressful events to prevent stress accumulation. Give a specific example of handling stress well. Distinguish between beneficial stress (performance-enhancing) and detrimental stress.
KEY POINTS:
- Pre-flight preparation reduces in-flight stress (anticipate, not react)
- Controlled breathing and systematic thinking during high-workload moments
- Physical fitness, sleep, and nutrition as stress buffers
- Debrief after stressful events (process, don't ruminate)
- Recognize and manage stress in crewmembers (CRM responsibility)
AVOID: "I don't get stressed." Not having any specific techniques.

Q: Explain your decision-making process for difficult decisions.
EXPECTED: Use a named framework: FORDEC (Facts, Options, Risks, Decide, Execute, Check) or TDODAR (Time, Diagnose, Options, Decide, Act, Review) — both are industry-accepted. For non-time-critical decisions: gather all available data, consult crew and relevant parties (dispatch, maintenance), assess options and risks, make a decision, execute, and monitor the outcome. For time-critical: rapid prioritization, execute memory items, then move to checklist. Always communicate the decision clearly to all stakeholders.
KEY POINTS:
- Reference FORDEC or TDODAR by name (shows professional education)
- Time-available: gather data, involve crew, assess options, decide
- Time-critical: memory items first, checklist follows
- Communication: always communicate the decision to all parties
- Review: check if the decision achieved the intended outcome
AVOID: "I just trust my gut." A process without any communication component.

Q: Would you help load the aircraft if ground crew is short-handed?
EXPECTED: Yes — and enthusiastically. This is a culture-fit question, especially for airlines like Southwest where the team culture means everyone pitches in. The answer should be: absolutely, as long as it doesn't interfere with your safety responsibilities (you can't skip the pre-flight inspection to load bags). Add: you understand that a delayed departure due to insufficient loading is a problem for passengers, and if helping gets the flight out on time, that's part of being a team member.
KEY POINTS:
- Yes, absolutely — team player mentality
- Caveat: safety responsibilities come first (pre-flight, W&B cannot be skipped)
- Shows Southwest culture alignment (or any collaborative airline culture)
- Demonstrates that you're not "above" physical work
AVOID: Saying "that's not my job." Any answer that sounds entitled or superior.

Q: What would you do if you witnessed sexual harassment?
EXPECTED: Don't be passive. Intervene safely (direct intervention if safe; distraction technique; enlist others; document). Report to HR or the appropriate channel. Support the victim. The answer should convey: you have a moral obligation not just a reporting obligation; bystander behavior makes you complicit; airline culture depends on everyone enforcing professional standards. You do not wait for someone else to handle it.
KEY POINTS:
- Intervene directly if safe; use distraction if not
- Support the victim immediately
- Report to HR/management through appropriate channels
- Document what you witnessed
- Do not dismiss it as "just a joke" or someone else's problem
AVOID: "I'd mind my own business." "I'd let HR handle it" without any personal intervention.

Q: What would you do if you saw a colleague behaving unethically?
EXPECTED: Direct but measured response. First: clarify — could there be a misunderstanding? Second: if confirmed unethical: address with the colleague directly (most effective first step). Third: if serious or not corrected: report through appropriate channels (chief pilot, HR, union EAP). For safety-related ethics violations: report to the FAA or airline safety hotline. The key message: you don't look the other way, and you use proper channels rather than taking unilateral action.
KEY POINTS:
- Address directly with the colleague first (most ethical violations resolve here)
- If safety-related: immediate action through safety reporting system
- If serious misconduct: management and HR
- Document the behavior and your response
- Loyalty to the profession is not blind loyalty to individuals
AVOID: Looking the other way. Going immediately to management without direct conversation (unless severity warrants immediate escalation).

Q: Do you have any questions for us?
EXPECTED: YES — always have 3-5 genuine questions prepared. This is NOT optional. Great questions: What does success look like in the first year for a new pilot joining your operation? How would you describe the training culture here — is there a Just Culture environment for mistakes? What does a typical progression look like from new hire to line captain? What do you think is the most exciting change coming to the airline in the next 3-5 years? What are you most proud of about this airline's safety culture? What should I know about this airline that I wouldn't find on the website?
KEY POINTS:
- Always have questions — never say "No, I think you've covered everything"
- Questions should be thoughtful, demonstrating research
- Ask about culture, training, progression, or the airline's future
- Do NOT ask about pay, benefits, or schedule
- Do NOT ask questions that were already answered in the interview
AVOID: Having no questions. Asking about pay or scheduling. Questions that show you didn't research the airline.

Q: What does a positive attitude mean for an airline pilot?
EXPECTED: Positive attitude in the cockpit means: approaching each flight with engaged professionalism regardless of fatigue or schedule disruption; being a constructive crew member who raises morale rather than draining it; handling irregular operations with equanimity; maintaining the crew's problem-solving orientation under stress; being a positive interface with passengers; representing the airline as an ambassador. It does NOT mean toxic positivity — ignoring problems. It means maintaining effectiveness and forward momentum even when things are hard.
KEY POINTS:
- Engaged professionalism regardless of circumstances
- Constructive crew energy — raises others's effectiveness
- Positive interface with passengers (PA tone, interaction quality)
- Solution-oriented when problems arise (not complaining)
- Professional optimism — confidence that challenges are manageable
- Distinguishes genuine positive attitude from ignoring real problems
AVOID: "Just being happy all the time." Missing the professional/safety dimension.

Q: What sets you apart from other candidates?
EXPECTED: Be specific and confident without being arrogant. Answer the question: what combination of experience, skill, character, and professional values make you the right choice for this role? The differentiating elements might be: prior military background and leadership training, exceptional hours in a specific aircraft type, prior career experience in another safety-critical field, documented history of speaking up for safety, outstanding CRM skills evidenced by specific examples. This is not the time for modesty — give a direct, evidence-based answer.
KEY POINTS:
- Specific differentiating experience (aircraft type, hours, military/industry background)
- Specific character trait backed by evidence
- Long-term commitment signal (demonstrating you're not just interviewing everywhere)
- Cultural alignment beyond what's in the resume
AVOID: "I'm very hardworking." Generic positive adjectives. Comparing yourself to other candidates negatively.

Q: How do you act as a positive role model on and off duty?
EXPECTED: On duty: consistent SOP adherence (even when no one is watching), professional demeanor, constructive crew interaction, mentoring junior crew members, quality PA delivery. Off duty: representing the airline with professional appearance and conduct in uniform, responsible behavior (alcohol — the 8-hour rule is a minimum, not a guideline for professionals), social media awareness, conducting yourself as an ambassador of the aviation profession even when not in uniform.
KEY POINTS:
- On duty: SOP adherence without oversight, positive crew energy, mentoring
- In uniform: professional appearance and conduct in public
- Off duty: alcohol responsibility (the 8-hour rule is a floor, not a target)
- Social media: representing the profession positively; no airline-damaging posts
- Community: engagement in aviation education, mentoring young pilots
AVOID: "I follow the rules." Missing the off-duty dimension.

Q: How do you approach continuous learning and professional development?
EXPECTED: Aviation requires lifelong learning — regulations evolve, aircraft change, new threats emerge. Show: you have active learning habits beyond required recurrent training; you seek feedback; you study accident reports (NTSB); you pursue relevant certificates and ratings proactively; you participate in professional organizations. Also show: you take recurrent training seriously as a learning opportunity, not just a compliance exercise. The most professional pilots treat every checkride as a chance to improve, not just pass.
KEY POINTS:
- Active learning beyond recurrent training minimums
- NTSB accident reports as learning tools
- Safety seminars, webinars, and industry publications
- Seeking feedback proactively from checkairmen and peers
- Professional organization participation (ALPA, FAPA, type clubs)
- Treating checkrides as improvement opportunities
AVOID: "I do my recurrent training every year." Not having any proactive learning habit.

Q: Tell me about a checkride failure — what happened and what did you learn?
EXPECTED: Identical guidance to Section 2 question. Honest, specific, ownership-focused. If you have no checkride failure: "I have not failed a checkride, but I've had debrief items that required corrective training — and those have been some of my most valuable learning experiences." If you have a failure: own it, explain the specific deficiency, describe the corrective training, describe the changed preparation approach, and articulate what the experience taught you about your learning process and resilience.
KEY POINTS:
- Honest and specific account
- Ownership with no blame-shifting
- The corrective training taken
- Changed preparation habits
- How it made you a better pilot / more resilient
AVOID: Hiding or minimizing failures. Blaming the examiner. Not demonstrating growth.

Q: Explain any gaps in your logbook.
EXPECTED: Interviewers ask this if they see periods of reduced or no flying. Legitimate reasons: medical certificate issue (be honest and explain resolution), military deployment, maternity/paternity leave, career transition, financial constraints during training. Always frame it as: what caused the gap, how you maintained your knowledge during the gap, and how you returned to currency. Document your recency and verify currency before the interview.
KEY POINTS:
- Honest explanation of the gap cause
- What you did to maintain knowledge/stay connected to aviation during the gap
- How you regained currency after the gap
- Any special skills or experience gained during the gap period
AVOID: Being evasive. Not having documentation of how you returned to currency.

Q: Explain any period of reduced flying or career change.
EXPECTED: Career changes are increasingly common in aviation — prior military, finance, engineering, medicine, or other careers before aviation are often strengths. Frame the career change as: additional skills that make you a better pilot, evidence of a deliberate decision to pursue aviation, and the commitment shown by making the transition. If flying was reduced: explain why and how you maintained or rebuilt proficiency.
KEY POINTS:
- Honest and direct explanation
- How the prior career added skills relevant to aviation (leadership, discipline, technical)
- The deliberate decision to transition (not a random path)
- What you did to maintain aviation proficiency during the transition
AVOID: Being defensive. Treating your prior career as something to hide.

Q: Have you ever had an FAA contact or violation?
EXPECTED: If no: say so directly and simply. "No, I have not had any FAA contact or violations in my career." If yes: full transparency is mandatory — the FAA has your records and so does the airline via the PRIA (Pilot Records Improvement Act) database. Describe what happened, take ownership, explain the outcome, and describe what you learned and changed. A violation that was resolved properly years ago is far less damaging than being caught being dishonest about it.
KEY POINTS:
- If no: simple direct statement
- If yes: fully transparent, specific account
- Context without excuse-making
- Outcome and resolution
- What you changed
AVOID: Any dishonesty or omission — they have your records. Minimizing a real violation.

Q: Have you ever been dismissed from a job?
EXPECTED: Same principle: full transparency. If no: say so. If yes: be honest, provide context (company layoffs, performance issues, disagreement with management), take appropriate ownership, describe what you learned, and explain what you would do differently. Note: you do not have to admit to things that didn't actually happen. But a truthful answer about a past employment problem is always better than being caught in inconsistency.
KEY POINTS:
- Simple direct statement if no
- If yes: honest, specific, ownership-oriented
- Context and what you learned
- What you changed as a result
AVOID: Dishonesty. Blaming the employer entirely. Failing to take any ownership.

Q: How do you define CRM and why is it important?
EXPECTED: CRM (Crew Resource Management) is the effective use of ALL available resources — hardware, software, and human — to achieve safe and efficient flight operations. Human resources include: flight crew, cabin crew, ATC, dispatch, maintenance. Key elements: situational awareness, communication, leadership and followership, decision-making, workload management, team building, stress management. CRM is important because human factors are the primary cause of aviation accidents — approximately 70-80% involve human error. CRM training emerged from analysis of accidents (Tenerife 1977, United 173 1978, Air Florida 90 1982) where communication and authority gradient failures were causal.
KEY POINTS:
- CRM: effective use of ALL available resources for safe, efficient operations
- Resources: people (crew, ATC, dispatch, maintenance) + systems + information
- Key elements: SA, communication, leadership, decision-making, workload management
- Importance: 70-80% of accidents involve human factors
- Historical driver: Tenerife 1977 (communication failure), UA173 (fuel exhaustion from authority gradient)
AVOID: Defining CRM as only "crew communication." Missing the all-resources definition.

Q: Explain the STAR method and how you use it.
EXPECTED: STAR = Situation, Task, Action, Result. Southwest also uses START (adding Takeaway). It's the structured format for answering behavioral interview questions. Situation: describe the context — who, what, where, when. Task: your specific role and responsibility. Action: the specific steps you personally took (use "I," not "we"). Result: the measurable or observable outcome. Good STAR answers are: specific (not hypothetical), concise (90-120 seconds), relevant to the competency being tested, and end with a positive result or a clear lesson learned. Practice STAR stories in advance — have 8-10 ready to adapt to different questions.
KEY POINTS:
- S: Situation — specific context setting
- T: Task — your role and what was at stake
- A: Action — what YOU specifically did (not "we")
- R: Result — measurable outcome or clear lesson
- START: Southwest adds Takeaway (T) at the end
- Keep to 90-120 seconds; practice out loud
AVOID: Using "we" throughout instead of "I." Stories that are vague or hypothetical. Missing the result/outcome. Stories longer than 2-3 minutes.

Q: If you could ask us one question about the airline that would help you decide, what would it be?
EXPECTED: This is the closing question and a test of how seriously you've taken the interview. Have a genuine, thoughtful question. Great options: "What do you think distinguishes your safety culture from other major carriers — what would I notice on Day 1?" or "What are the qualities you've seen in the most successful pilots who joined your airline in the last five years?" or "What aspect of working here most surprised pilots when they arrived from other airlines?" Shows you've thought deeply about the decision and are serious about the fit.
KEY POINTS:
- The question should be genuinely meaningful to your decision
- Not about pay, benefits, or schedule
- Shows you've researched and thought about what really matters
- Should reflect the values you've expressed throughout the interview (safety, culture, growth)
AVOID: "No, I think you've covered everything." A question about compensation. A question that was already answered.
