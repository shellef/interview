=== WHAT WWYD (WHAT WOULD YOU DO) QUESTIONS TEST ===

Airlines use WWYD/scenario questions to assess:
- Crew Resource Management (CRM) — do you communicate, collaborate, escalate?
- Decision-making under pressure — do you use a structured process?
- Safety culture — will you speak up even to a captain?
- Authority gradient management — assertive but not aggressive
- Procedural adherence vs. judgment

Key principle: "What is RIGHT, not WHO is right."

Assessors evaluate against NOTECHS competencies:
1. Cooperation — how you work with crew
2. Leadership and management — how you handle authority
3. Situational awareness — how you read situations
4. Decision-making — how you reach courses of action

=== DECISION-MAKING FRAMEWORKS ===

FORDEC (used by Lufthansa group airlines):
F — Facts: What do we know?
O — Options: What are our choices?
R — Risks: What are the risks of each option?
D — Decision: What are we doing?
E — Execution: Do it
C — Check: Did it work? Reassess.

TDODAR (used by UK carriers):
T — Time: How much time do we have?
D — Diagnose: What is the problem?
O — Options: What are our options?
D — Decide: Choose a course of action
A — Act: Execute
R — Review: Check the outcome

=== GRADUATED ASSERTIVENESS MODEL ===

When dealing with a CRM issue with a captain, use graduated escalation:
1. QUERY — "I noticed we're below the minimum safe altitude — is that correct?"
2. ASSERT — State your concern clearly: "I'm not comfortable with this. We need to climb."
3. CHALLENGE — More direct: "Captain, we must climb now for terrain separation."
4. EMERGENCY — If all else fails and safety is compromised: "I'm declaring an emergency."

Most SOP deviations are unintentional — a clear, non-judgmental assertion resolves ~90% of cases.

=== 9 CORE SCENARIO THEMES WITH SAMPLE ANSWERS ===

--- SCENARIO 1: Captain making unsafe decision ---
"You're at 35,000 feet. The Captain decides to descend below minimum vectoring altitude because he 'knows the terrain like the back of his hand.' You're the FO. What do you do?"

Strong answer approach:
- Immediately query: "Captain, I show us below the minimum safe altitude here — I'm not comfortable with that. Can we verify?"
- Reference the chart/data, not personal preference
- Escalate if necessary: "I need to call it out — this doesn't look safe to me."
- If he ignores you: "Captain, I'm going to need to make a call to ATC."
- Never: silently comply; argue aggressively; just take over the controls

Key elements: Stay calm, use data, communicate clearly, escalate professionally.

--- SCENARIO 2: Captain deviating from SOP ---
"Your captain consistently skips a checklist item because he says it's unnecessary. What do you do?"

Strong answer approach:
- First occurrence: politely draw attention to it ("I don't see us getting through that checklist item — want me to call it out?")
- Pattern: address it directly but respectfully, one-on-one before the flight
- Not improving: document, consider ASAP/SMS report, involve chief pilot
- Never: ignore it; confront publicly; threaten immediately

--- SCENARIO 3: Unstabilized approach ---
"You're at 500 feet, unstabilized, captain is continuing the approach. What do you do?"

Strong answer:
- "Unstabilized" call-out immediately per SOPs
- If no response: "Going around" — call it and initiate
- You have the authority and responsibility to call a go-around
- Document afterward; debrief calmly

--- SCENARIO 4: Weather deterioration ---
"You're on final, weather is now below minimums. Captain wants to continue. What do you do?"

Strong answer:
- Reference the minimums clearly: "Our minimums are 200/half — current ATIS shows 100 and RVR 600."
- "I don't think we're legal to continue — we need to go missed."
- If captain disagrees: firmly state you cannot continue; escalate as needed
- Discuss FORDEC or TDODAR framework — time allows for brief process

--- SCENARIO 5: Intoxicated captain ---
"You show up for a flight and your captain appears to be impaired/intoxicated. What do you do?"

Strong answer:
- Do NOT fly — this is a bright line
- Address privately and respectfully: "I'm concerned about how you're doing today — I don't think we should fly."
- Contact operations/chief pilot immediately
- Document everything
- Your career concern is secondary to safety

--- SCENARIO 6: Pilot incapacitation ---
"Halfway through the flight, your captain becomes incapacitated. What do you do?"

Strong answer:
- Take the controls immediately
- Secure the incapacitated pilot (seatbelt, move if possible)
- Declare emergency with ATC
- Brief cabin crew (PAN PAN or MAYDAY)
- Divert to nearest suitable airport
- Get assistance from senior cabin crew or deadheading pilot if available
- Follow incapacitation checklist

--- SCENARIO 7: Fuel dilemma ---
"You're 45 minutes from destination, weather has deteriorated, your alternate is also marginal, and you're at minimum fuel. What do you do?"

Strong answer:
- Declare minimum fuel to ATC (not yet emergency, but priority handling)
- Request direct routing to destination
- Assess: if destination unlikely, declare MAYDAY fuel emergency
- Better to declare early — never let fuel become an emergency if it can be avoided
- Discuss options systematically (FORDEC)

--- SCENARIO 8: Crew conflict ---
"Your FO and a flight attendant are in a heated argument before boarding. What do you do?"

Strong answer:
- Separate them immediately, address privately
- As captain: you have authority and responsibility for crew harmony
- Assess whether either is fit to fly (emotional state)
- Delay departure if necessary
- Brief both on professional expectations
- Document; debrief after flight

--- SCENARIO 9: Fatigue ---
"You're on day 4 of a trip, exhausted, legal to fly but not feeling alert. Captain says to push through. What do you do?"

Strong answer:
- You have the authority and obligation to call fatigue
- "I need to be honest — I'm not feeling fit to fly safely today."
- Fatigue call is protected under most airline policies and SMS
- Document the fatigue
- Discuss with captain and operations
- Do NOT fly when you feel impaired by fatigue

=== WHAT INTERVIEWERS ARE WATCHING FOR ===

RED FLAGS (will get you rejected):
- Jumping straight to action without process/communication
- Undermining captain's authority aggressively
- Complete passivity — just going along with unsafe behavior
- Hero narrative — "I would just take over"
- Ignoring crew, going straight to ATC without communication
- Treating it as a binary decision without exploring options

GREEN FLAGS (what they want to see):
- Systematic approach (use a framework)
- Communication first — query, assert, challenge
- References to specific SOPs/regulations
- Calm, professional demeanor even in extremis
- Appropriate escalation ladder
- Willingness to be the bad guy for safety
- Documentation and post-event debrief awareness

=== COMMON WWYD QUESTIONS ACROSS AIRLINES ===

1. Captain arrives with alcohol on breath — what do you do?
2. You're cleared for an approach below minimums — what do you do?
3. Dispatch is pressuring you to depart with questionable MEL item — what do you do?
4. ATC clears you to an altitude that would cause TCAS conflict — what do you do?
5. Your FO is flying, clearly fatigued and making errors — what do you do?
6. Passenger is combative and needs to be removed — what do you do?
7. Maintenance says the aircraft is airworthy but you're not satisfied — what do you do?
8. Mid-flight, your FO disagrees strongly with your routing decision — how do you handle it?
9. You see another aircraft on a collision course and ATC hasn't called traffic — what do you do?
10. Engine failure on takeoff at V1 — walk me through what you do.
11. Rapid decompression at FL350 — what are your immediate actions?
12. Windshear warning on approach — what do you do?
