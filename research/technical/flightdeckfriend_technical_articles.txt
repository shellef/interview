=== HOW TO PASS AN AIRLINE PILOT INTERVIEW ===
SOURCE: https://www.flightdeckfriend.com/pilot-career-hub/how-to-pass-an-airline-pilot-interview

Airline interviews usually consist of two parts: a competency assessment and a technical assessment.

The competency-based interview is a "tick box" exercise — the assessor has boxes to tick to show you demonstrated required competencies. If you tick all boxes, you get the job. If you fail to show required attributes, the application cannot progress.

TOP 15 ATTRIBUTES AIRLINES ASSESS:
1. Problem-Solving
2. Situational Awareness
3. Team Work
4. Leadership
5. Prioritisation
6. Delegation
7. Communication
8. Planning
9. Flexibility
10. Reliability
11. Empathy
12. Business Orientated
13. Customer Minded
14. Adverse to risk
15. Motivated / Passionate

FIRST IMPRESSIONS:
- Well-dressed: plain dark suit, neutral tie, polished shoes, smart briefcase
- Trimmed nails, freshly cut hair, cleanly shaved
- Greet everyone with "good morning" and warm smile — receptionist to CEO
- Firm handshake, eye contact
- Sit upright, hands on lap, open body language — not arms crossed

INTERVIEW TIPS:
- If assessor keeps asking same question differently, they're trying to help you demonstrate a competency — adjust your answer to show that attribute
- If you can't think of an answer, ask to come back to it. If still stuck, say what you WOULD do.
- Be confident — the role requires it
- Never lie — you'll contradict yourself or be caught out by body language
- You're being interviewed for future Captain, not just First Officer

KNOW THE AIRLINE — be able to answer:
- Aircraft types (engines, pax config, MTOW, VMO, max ceiling)
- Fleet size
- Destinations and bases
- Orders (new aircraft on order)
- History (when formed, key milestones)
- Key people (CEO, Director of Flight Operations, Chief Pilot)
- Financial overview (profit, revenue, passengers carried, growth)
- Recent news (new routes, new aircraft)
- Biggest competitors
- Industry threats (oil price, competition, global events)

WHAT TO BRING:
- Flying Licence
- Class One Medical Certificate
- Logbook (tabbed, professional)
- Confirmation of interview
- Pens and notepad
- Academic qualification certificates
- Flight school final report (if applicable)
- Application summary

DRESS CODE:
DO wear: Black/dark suit (matching jacket and trousers), ironed white shirt, conservative tie (Windsor knot), highly polished black shoes, black belt, smart conservative watch, conservative haircut
DO NOT wear: Non-dark suit, brightly coloured tie, messy facial hair/stubble, visible tattoos, piercings (males)

EXAMPLE QUESTIONS (free samples from their database):
- Tell me about the typical day of a pilot?
- What makes a good first officer?
- What challenges does the company face over the next 10 years?
- Tell us about a time you've fallen short at something?
- Explain Dutch Roll?
- How do you calculate the required sweep of the wings?
- Give me an example of when you have demonstrated strong leadership skills?
- When have you worked well within a team?
- How do you deal with conflict?

=== WHAT IS V1 SPEED? ===
SOURCE: https://www.flightdeckfriend.com/ask-a-pilot/what-is-v1-speed-for-a-commercial-aircraft/

V1 has TWO meanings:
1. The speed by which the FIRST ACTION must have been taken to reject the takeoff if the aircraft is to safely stop on the remaining runway (Accelerate-Stop Distance Available / ASDA)
2. The MINIMUM speed at which the aircraft is 'guaranteed' to get airborne and reach screen height (35ft dry / 15ft wet) in the remaining takeoff distance available if an engine fails

FAA: First action to stop = reducing thrust OR applying brakes OR deploying speedbrake.

KEY RULE: If engine fails BELOW V1 and takeoff is continued, aircraft may not get airborne in remaining distance. If takeoff is REJECTED ABOVE V1, aircraft may not stop on remaining runway.

DECISION TIMING: V1 is no longer called "go/no-go" — the decision must be made BEFORE V1. There is a 2-second buffer built in as safety margin.

V1 MUST be: greater than VMCG and less than VR.

FACTORS AFFECTING V1:
- Takeoff Distance Available (TODA/ASDA)
- Flap setting
- Pressure altitude (temperature, pressure, field elevation)
- Aircraft weight
- Runway surface state (dry/wet/contaminated)
- Runway slope
- Wind speed and direction

TYPICAL RANGE: 120-140 knots for commercial jets (B737, A320, B747, A380).

ON THE FLIGHT DECK: Pilot monitoring calls "V1". Pilot flying (or Captain at some airlines) removes hands from thrust levers at V1 call — prevents instinctive rejection after V1.

=== WHY DO PLANES ABORT LANDINGS (GO-AROUND)? ===
SOURCE: https://www.flightdeckfriend.com/ask-a-pilot/why-do-planes-abort-landings/

Go-arounds occur about 1-3 times per 1,000 approaches. Well-briefed, practiced manoeuvre.

TERMINOLOGY:
- Go-around: discontinuing approach before flare, taking aircraft back up
- Rejected landing: decision made after flare has commenced (~30ft)
- Baulked landing: same as rejected landing — gear may have touched
- Discontinued approach: passenger-friendly term pilots use in PA

COMMON CAUSES:
1. ATC instruction — spacing too close, blocked runway
2. Weather: sudden wind speed/direction change; strong tailwind (>15kts); strong crosswind (>35-40kts); cloud base below minimums; visibility below minimums (fog, mist, snow, rain)
3. Unstabilised approach — airlines require stabilisation by 1,000ft IMC / 500ft VMC:
   - Aircraft in landing config (gear down, landing flap)
   - Correct target speed
   - Landing checklist complete
   - Correct vertical profile (typically 3° descent, ~700-800 fpm)
   - Correct lateral profile
   If any not met: GO AROUND
4. Technical problem — affecting stopping distance
5. Deep landing — aircraft likely to miss touchdown zone

=== HOW MUCH FUEL ARE AIRCRAFT REQUIRED TO CARRY? ===
SOURCE: https://www.flightdeckfriend.com/ask-a-pilot/how-much-fuel-are-aircraft-required-to-carry/

EASA MINIMUM FUEL REQUIREMENTS (FAA broadly similar):

1. TRIP FUEL — fuel from start of takeoff to touchdown at destination
2. DIVERSION FUEL — fuel from go-around at destination to landing at alternate (or 15 min holding at 1,500ft if no alternate planned)
3. RESERVE FUEL — 30 min holding at 1,500ft at planned landing weight at alternate (or destination if no alternate)
4. CONTINGENCY FUEL — higher of:
   - 5% of trip fuel (or 3% if en-route alternate selected)
   - 20 minutes at planned trip fuel consumption
   - Statistical Contingency Fuel (SCF)
   - 5 minutes at holding speed at 1,500ft
5. TAXI FUEL — APU burn, engine start, taxi out (statistical)
6. ADDITIONAL FUEL — if required for engine failure/depressurisation at most critical point: descent + trip to alternate + 15 min hold + approach and land

FINAL DECISION: Captain is always responsible for fuel load. Discusses with FO. Common reasons for extra fuel: weather, ATC delays, increased passenger numbers, technical defect.

=== WHAT HAPPENS IF ALL ENGINES FAIL? ===
SOURCE: https://www.flightdeckfriend.com/ask-a-pilot/total-engine-failure

Aircraft WILL GLIDE — won't simply fall out of sky.
- Lift-to-drag ratio of ~10:1 for commercial jets
- At 36,000ft (6 miles): can glide ~60 miles
- 20-30 minutes of flying time to find somewhere to land
- During normal descent, engines are at idle = effectively gliding already

CAUSES OF DUAL ENGINE FAILURE:
- Bird strike (US Airways 1549 — Hudson River; 737 at Rome Ciampino 2008)
- Shutting down wrong engine (British Midland Flight 92, Kegworth 1989 — 47 killed)
- Fuel starvation/leak (Air Transat 236 — Azores 2001; Air Canada 143 — fuel miscalculation 1983)
- Fuel icing (BA Flight 38, Heathrow 2008 — Boeing 777 fuel system ice)
- Flame out in extreme turbulence/precipitation (can be restarted)
- Volcanic ash (BA Flight 9, 1982 — Boeing 747 lost all 4 engines, restarted outside ash cloud)
- Engine separation (shear pin design — protects structure)

=== WHAT HAPPENS IF A PLANE FLIES TOO HIGH? ===
SOURCE: https://www.flightdeckfriend.com/ask-a-pilot/what-happens-if-a-passenger-jet-flies-too-high

COFFIN CORNER: ~40,000-45,000ft (varies with weight and conditions)
- Low-speed stall and high-speed buffet meet
- Cannot speed up, slow down, or climb
- Only option: descend

THREE LIMITING FACTORS FOR MAXIMUM ALTITUDE:

1. ENGINE THRUST — air too thin to produce sufficient thrust for 300 fpm climb. Hot days = lower max altitude (less dense air).

2. CABIN PRESSURE DIFFERENTIAL — max ~9 PSI differential between cabin and outside. Reached at ~43,000ft. Above this: structural failure risk.

3. COFFIN CORNER / AERODYNAMIC ALTITUDE — 1.3g safety margin applied. Varies with aircraft weight.

=== WHY DO PLANES FLY SO HIGH? ===
SOURCE: https://www.flightdeckfriend.com/ask-a-pilot/why-do-planes-fly-so-high/

Typical cruise altitude: 30,000-42,000ft

TWO MAIN REASONS:

1. ENGINE EFFICIENCY: Turbofan engines most efficient at near-maximum RPM/temperature. At low altitude, max thrust at level flight would overspeed the aircraft. At altitude, thinner air means less thrust for same TAS — engines run near their efficient limits.

2. REDUCED DRAG: Thinner air = less aerodynamic drag = less thrust needed = less fuel burned. Aircraft travels faster per unit of fuel at altitude.

BONUS: Above ~30,000ft, above most weather systems = smoother, more comfortable for passengers.

=== HOW DO PLANES FLY? ===
SOURCE: https://www.flightdeckfriend.com/ask-a-pilot/how-do-planes-fly

FOUR FORCES OF FLIGHT:
- Lift (upward) — must exceed Weight
- Weight (downward, through CG)
- Thrust (forward) — must exceed Drag
- Drag (rearward, opposing motion)

LIFT — depends on:
- Speed of airflow over wing (faster = more lift)
- Wing size and shape (flaps/slats make wing bigger and more curved)
- Angle of attack (pitch attitude)
- Air density

HOW LIFT IS GENERATED:
Wing is more curved on top than bottom. Air over top travels further but takes same time → flows faster. Faster flow = lower pressure above wing. Lower pressure "sucks" aircraft upward (Bernoulli principle + Newton's 3rd law).

TAKEOFF SPEED: ~120-150 mph (depending on weight, config, conditions)

PRACTICAL: Flaps and slats used for takeoff/landing — make wing bigger and more curved, generate more lift at slow speeds.
