=== TECHNICAL INTERVIEW OVERVIEW ===

Format: 20-40 minutes of rapid-fire technical questions. Face-to-face or video.
Topics: Aircraft systems, meteorology, navigation, performance, regulations, CRM.
Key advice: Clear explanations matter more than reciting textbook wording. Show practical understanding.

=== AIRSPACE & REGULATIONS ===

Q: What is max airspeed in Class C airspace?
A: 200 knots indicated within 4 nautical miles of the primary airport, below 2,500 AGL.

Q: At what altitude does the 250-knot speed restriction end?
A: 10,000 MSL.

Q: What is needed to descend below DA/DH on an ILS?
A: Per FAR 91.175 — required flight visibility, AND one of the specified visual references (approach lights, threshold, touchdown zone, etc.) must be visible. Must be in position for a normal landing.

Q: Can you depart under 121 when weather is below landing minimums?
A: Yes, but you must file an alternate airport.

Q: When is a position report to ATC required on an IFR flight in VFR conditions?
A: FAR 91.183 — you are responsible for IFR separation. Required reports (MARVELOUS CVFR 500 mnemonic):
  M - Missed approach
  A - Airspeed changes of 5% or 10 knots (whichever greater)
  R - Reaching a holding fix
  V - VFR on top
  E - Leaving assigned altitude
  L - Leaving holding fix
  O - Outer marker inbound (non-radar)
  U - Unforecast weather
  S - Safety of flight
  C - Compulsory reporting points
  VFR - Entering/leaving VFR-on-top
  F - FAF inbound (non-radar)
  R - Radio/nav failure
  500 - Altitude deviation of 300 feet

Q: What is an LDA approach?
A: A non-precision approach using an ILS localizer but offset from the runway centerline. Provides lateral guidance only. Used when runway alignment prevents a straight-in ILS.

Q: What is the difference between a Category I, II, and III ILS?
A: Cat I: DH 200 ft, RVR 1800 ft (or ½ SM visibility)
   Cat II: DH 100 ft, RVR 1200 ft
   Cat IIIa: DH <100 ft or no DH, RVR ≥700 ft
   Cat IIIb: DH <50 ft or no DH, RVR 150-700 ft
   Cat IIIc: No DH, no RVR (full autoland, rare)

Q: What is RNAV / RNP?
A: RNAV (Area Navigation) allows aircraft to fly any desired flight path using GPS/FMS rather than flying over ground-based navaids. RNP (Required Navigation Performance) adds onboard monitoring and alerting — the aircraft must be able to confirm it's within a specified accuracy (e.g., RNP 0.1 nm).

Q: What is ETOPS and what does ETOPS 180 mean?
A: Extended Twin Operations. ETOPS 180 means a twin-engine aircraft can fly routes where the diversion to an alternate is up to 180 minutes at single-engine cruise speed. Requires specific maintenance, crew training, and dispatch requirements.

=== AERODYNAMICS ===

Q: Explain the four forces of flight.
A: Lift (opposes weight, generated by wings), Weight (gravity, acts downward through CG), Thrust (generated by engines, opposes drag), Drag (opposes thrust, parallel to flight path). In straight-and-level unaccelerated flight, lift=weight, thrust=drag.

Q: What is the difference between parasite drag and induced drag?
A: Parasite drag increases with speed (form drag, skin friction, interference drag).
   Induced drag is a byproduct of lift generation — decreases as speed increases. Caused by spanwise airflow creating wingtip vortices.
   Total drag curve: induced dominates at low speed, parasite at high speed. Best glide/L/D max is where they intersect.

Q: What are winglets for?
A: Reduce induced drag by disrupting wingtip vortices, improving lift-to-drag ratio. Improve fuel efficiency, particularly on long-haul flights. Also increase effective wing span without increasing physical span.

Q: What is coffin corner?
A: At high altitude, the stall speed (increases with altitude due to lower air density requiring higher TAS) and MMO (max operating Mach, limited by buffet/control issues) converge into a narrow band. The aircraft has very little speed margin — flying too slow = stall, too fast = Mach buffet. Typical at 40,000+ feet.

Q: What is Mach tuck / tuck-under?
A: At high Mach numbers, a shock wave forms on the wing causing the center of pressure to shift aft. This increases the downward force on the tail, causing a nose-down pitching moment (tuck). The aircraft pitches down, increasing speed, worsening the problem.

Q: What is critical Mach?
A: The lowest Mach number at which airflow over any part of the aircraft reaches the speed of sound. Above Mcrit, shock waves begin forming. This is typically 0.72-0.82 for commercial transports.

Q: How does aft CG affect aircraft performance?
A: Aft CG (within limits) improves performance — less trim drag required since the stabilizer doesn't need to push down as much. However, aft CG reduces longitudinal stability and can make recovery from unusual attitudes more difficult. Forward CG is safer but less efficient.

Q: What factors affect aircraft performance calculations?
A: Temperature (density altitude), pressure altitude, humidity, wind, runway slope, runway surface condition, aircraft weight, anti-ice bleed air extraction.

=== METEOROLOGY ===

Q: What is a microburst?
A: An intense localized downdraft from a convective cell that spreads out at the surface. Creates a severe windshear — headwind increasing on approach (aircraft climbs above glidepath), then tailwind as you pass through the core (aircraft sinks below glidepath). Can cause loss of control during approach/departure. Recognize: Pireps, weather radar returns, wind-shear alerts. Action: Reject landing/takeoff, apply windshear escape maneuver (TOGA power, rotate to initial climb attitude, fly airspeed, don't chase glidepath).

Q: What is the correlation between temperature and lightning?
A: More lightning occurs in higher temperature environments due to greater convective energy. Warmer, more unstable air masses create stronger updrafts and greater charge separation in thunderstorms.

Q: What is clear air turbulence (CAT)?
A: Turbulence found in clear air (no clouds), typically associated with jet streams and wind shear at high altitudes. Found near tropopause. Cannot be detected by weather radar — relies on PIREPs, METARs at nearby stations, turbulence forecasts (SIGMETs).

Q: What is BR on a METAR?
A: Mist. Visibility 5/8 mile to 6 miles.

Q: Describe high and low pressure systems.
A: High pressure: sinking air, diverging at surface, clear skies, stable conditions, clockwise flow in N hemisphere.
   Low pressure: rising air that cools and condenses, converging at surface, clouds and precipitation, counterclockwise flow in N hemisphere.

Q: What is a SIGMET and when is it issued?
A: Significant Meteorological information. Issued for conditions hazardous to all aircraft:
   - Severe/extreme turbulence
   - Severe icing not associated with thunderstorms
   - Widespread dust/sandstorms below 5,000 AGL
   - Volcanic ash
   Convective SIGMETs cover severe thunderstorms, embedded thunderstorms, lines of thunderstorms, areas with >40% coverage.

=== AIRCRAFT SYSTEMS ===

Q: Walk me through the electrical system of a jet transport.
A: Primary AC power from engine-driven generators (IDG - Integrated Drive Generator). AC bus feeds most aircraft systems. Transformer-Rectifier Units (TRUs) convert AC to DC for DC buses. APU can power electrical system on ground. External power available on ground. Battery provides emergency backup. Some aircraft have Ram Air Turbine (RAT) for hydraulic/electrical emergency power.

Q: Describe how a constant speed propeller works.
A: Uses a governor to maintain constant RPM. When load increases (climb), blade pitch flattens to maintain RPM. When load decreases (descent), pitch increases. Oil pressure acts against spring/counterweight to change pitch. Failure mode: typically goes to fine pitch (high RPM, low efficiency) — controllable.

Q: What is bleed air and what is it used for?
A: High-pressure, high-temperature air extracted from engine compressor stages. Used for: pressurization and air conditioning (packs), wing/engine anti-ice, hydraulic reservoir pressurization, water system pressurization. On newer aircraft like 787, electric systems replace bleed air (more efficient).

Q: What are the three primary flight control hydraulic systems typically on a large transport?
A: Most large transports (e.g., 737) have two main hydraulic systems (A and B). Some (e.g., 767) have three. Each powers different flight control surfaces redundantly. If one fails, flight controls remain operable. Some controls (e.g., ground spoilers) may require both systems.

Q: Describe the fuel system considerations for a long-haul flight.
A: Fuel planning includes: block fuel = trip fuel + alternate fuel + final reserve (30 min holding) + contingency (5% or 5 min). ETOPS requires additional fuel. Cross-feed valves allow fuel from any tank to any engine. CG management via fuel transfer. Fuel freeze point monitoring at altitude. Fuel quantity via capacitance probes.

=== V-SPEEDS ===

V1 — Takeoff decision speed. At V1, you are committed to fly. If engine failure occurs before V1, abort. After V1, continue.

VR — Rotation speed. Begin rotating aircraft.

V2 — Takeoff safety speed. Minimum speed to maintain with one engine failed after takeoff. Must reach V2 by 35 ft above runway.

VREF — Reference landing speed (typically 1.3 x Vstall in landing config). Final approach speed.

VA — Maneuvering speed. Do not apply full control inputs above VA.

VMO/MMO — Maximum operating speed (knots) / Mach. Do not exceed.

VFE — Maximum flap extended speed for each flap setting.

VLE / VLO — Maximum landing gear extended / operating speeds.

=== PERFORMANCE ===

Q: What is balanced field length?
A: The runway length where the accelerate-stop distance equals the accelerate-go distance at V1. The optimal V1 for minimum runway requirement. Affected by: weight, temperature, pressure altitude, runway slope.

Q: What is a stopway?
A: A prepared rectangular area at the end of a runway, same width as runway, able to support aircraft during an aborted takeoff. Can be used to increase accelerate-stop distance but NOT for accelerate-go (not a runway).

Q: What is a clearway?
A: An area beyond the runway end, at least 500 ft wide, cleared of obstructions. Can be credited for the first segment of obstacle clearance climb. Cannot be used for accelerate-stop distance.

=== INSTRUMENT PROCEDURES ===

Q: Walk me through briefing an approach plate.
A: Systematic coverage:
   1. Airport/approach identification (name, runway, type)
   2. Communication frequencies (ATIS, approach, tower, ground)
   3. Plan view — overall picture, IAF, transition routes
   4. Profile view — altitude constraints, stepdown fixes
   5. Final approach — course, FAF/FAP, DA/MDA, missed approach point
   6. Missed approach procedure — heading, altitude, fix
   7. Minimums — visibility, ceiling, alternate minimums
   8. Notes and special procedures
   9. Threat and error management

Q: What is a stabilized approach?
A: By 1,000 ft AAL in IMC (500 ft VMC):
   - On correct flight path (glidepath ±1 dot)
   - At correct speed (VREF +/- limits)
   - Correct configuration (gear, flaps)
   - Correct power setting
   - Correct checklist complete
   - Sink rate not exceeding 1,000 fpm (1,300 fpm acceptable briefly)
   If not stabilized, go around.

=== DUTY TIME / REGULATIONS (FAR Part 117) ===

Flight Duty Period (FDP) limits depend on: number of flight segments, time of day (WOCL — Window of Circadian Low, 0200-0559 local).

Maximum FDP: 9-14 hours depending on report time and segments.
Maximum flight time: 8-9 hours per FDP, 100 hours in 672 hours, 1,000 hours per 365 days.
Minimum rest: 10 hours, including 8 hours uninterrupted sleep opportunity.
